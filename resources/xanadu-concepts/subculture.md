# Subculture of Intellect

Source: Literary Machines, 3/16-3/18

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Nelson envisions Xanadu not merely as a technical system but as the foundation for a new intellectual community—a "subculture of intellect." This is distinct from both academia and science fiction fandom, though it shares characteristics with both: the enthusiasm and accessibility of fan culture combined with genuine intellectual substance.

The key insight is that Xanadu's value depends on creating a community that actively uses it. A network of information is only as valuable as the people who populate it and the culture they develop around exchanging knowledge. Nelson proposes deliberately constructing this subculture through "Xanadu Stands"—physical locations where the system is demonstrated and where a community can form.

### User Guarantee

You will not be alone. The system comes with a community of fellow users who share interests, exchange knowledge, and help each other navigate the information landscape.

### Principle Served

Technology alone is insufficient; systems succeed through the cultures that grow around them. Xanadu requires an engaged community of generalists who value interconnection over specialization, and who find joy in the exchange of ideas across traditional disciplinary boundaries.

### How Users Experience It

- Local "Xanadu Stands" staffed by knowledgeable "Hypercorps" who help users
- A traveling community of generalists who share insights and discoveries
- Festivals and events (Hypercons, Footnote Festivals, Intertwingling Expos)
- A culture that values knowing about everything—"like trivia freaks or D&D players"
- Social status based on breadth of knowledge and ability to make connections
- Always having a Xanadu screen nearby for reference during conversations

### The Xanadu Hypercorps

Nelson proposes a specific mechanism for building this subculture: the "Xanadu Hypercorps"—a national corps of knowledgeable people who staff Xanadu Stands. Their job is not to program computers but to help users be productive:

- "Like the stewards and stewardesses of the airlines, they will know how to make users comfortable"
- "Like good librarians... they will know how to deal with an avalanche, rather than a trickle, of ideas and information"
- "Like good teachers they will have a sense of how to convey ideas"
- "Like good woodsmen they will have a sense of the trails and byways of the territory to be explored"

The Hypercorps would circulate among Xanadu stations, transmitting skills and outlook, creating consistency across the network.

### Two Academic Subcultures (Contrast)

Nelson identifies two existing intellectual subcultures to distinguish Xanadu's approach:

1. **Traditional academic culture**: Connected to universities, with established prestige hierarchies and connections back to the Middle Ages. Examples: grand English, Harper's, The New Yorker.

2. **Science fiction/popular science**: A "new intellectual subculture" including science-fiction and popular science. Examples: Omni magazine, Buckminster Fuller, Isaac Asimov. Popular science magazines are its outposts; Wide Earth Review is its pinnacle.

Xanadu's subculture is meant to be distinct from both—combining the intellectual seriousness of academia with the enthusiasm and accessibility of fan culture.
