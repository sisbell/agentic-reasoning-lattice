# Windowing

Source: Literary Machines, Chapter 2 (2/32-2/40)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Windowing is **seeing through to another document**. A window in your document shows content from someone else's document, without copying it. The original stays in place, owned by its creator. You create a "view" - a transparent opening that lets readers see the other document's content as if it were part of yours.

This is Nelson's primary mechanism for what we now call transclusion. The key insight: your document is like a sheet of glass with some painted content (your words) and some clear areas (windows) where the reader sees through to other documents beneath.

### User Guarantee

When you create a windowing document:
- You see other people's content as part of your document
- The original owner retains full ownership and control
- Nothing is copied - the content exists only in the original
- Readers can "step through" the window into the original
- The original can change, and you see the changes (unless you fix the window to a point in time)

### Principle Served

**Compound documents without copying.** The problem with quotation by copying is threefold: copies diverge from originals, copies lose connection to their source, and copies create the problem of "distributed update" when the original changes.

Windowing solves all three:
- The original is the content - no divergence possible
- The connection is structural - always traceable
- Updates propagate automatically through windows

### How Users Experience It

Creating a windowing document:
- Quote from another document by creating a window to it
- Mix your own writing with windowed content
- Windows can be to specific locations OR to specific versions
- Build layered documents - a "window sandwich" of many levels

Reading a windowing document:
- See the composite as one document
- Know which parts are windows (visible attribution)
- Choose to "step through" a window into the original document
- Explore the original, then return through the "gateway"

Two types of windows:
- **Floating in time**: Shows current version of quoted material (updates visible)
- **Fixed in time**: Shows the version at a specific moment (frozen reference)

### Nelson's Words

On the core mechanism:
> "Someone else may create a document which quotes it as much as desired. This mechanism we call the quote-window or quote-link. Through a 'window' in the new document we see a portion of the old. We may also call this an inclusion."

On what a compound document is:
> "A compound document consists of parts of old documents together with new material. Or we may say it really consists of quote-links and new material."

On the glass pane metaphor:
> "Think of the present document as a sheet of glass. It may have writing painted on it by the present author; it may have clear glass, windowing to something else; the next pane may be in turn made of more layers of painted glass, with more windows, and so on indefinitely."

On navigating through windows:
> "Only when you step through the window--turning one glass page and going on in the next--do you reach the original that you wanted. But stepping through the window means you are now in another work."

On ownership across windows:
> "Document A can include Document B, even though Document B is owned by someone else."

On multi-level windowing:
> "A document may have a window to another document, and that one to yet another, indefinitely. Thus A contains part of B, and so on. One document can be built upon another, and yet another document can be built upon that one, indefinitely: each having links to what was already in place."

On the distributed update solution:
> "No copying operations are required among the documents throughout the system, and thus we solve the problems of update--especially the problem of updating documents which depend on other documents. We solve this problem simply by windowing to a changing document."

On two-way reading:
> "As already stated, it is possible for the reader to ask to see the materials which are windowed to by a given document. However, it must also be possible for the reader to ask to see whatever documents window to the current document. Both are available at any time."

On time-fixed vs location-fixed windows:
> "Note also, however, that a quotation--an inclusion window--may be fixed to another document in two ways: at a certain point in time, in which case revisions are seen by the user only when he or she asks, 'What has this passage become?' Or second, at a relatively fixed location in the document space, in which case updates are seen automatically."

On versioning by inclusion:
> "Another user, however, is free to create his or her own alternative version of the document he or she does not own. This, then, becomes a windowing document using the shared materials by including them. We may call this versioning by inclusion."

On derivative documents:
> "A particular form of compound document is one which consists explicitly of an original and changes to it--which may be made by anyone. (We may call this a derivative document.)"

On simplicity despite complexity:
> "This world nevertheless remains simple in design. The virtuality is simple in structure and repeats in layers. You always know where you are, and can at once ascertain the home document of any specific word or character."
