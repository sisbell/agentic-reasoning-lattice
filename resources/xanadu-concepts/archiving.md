# Archiving

Source: Literary Machines, 3/25 (page 167)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Xanadu is designed as a permanent archive for human civilization's intellectual heritage. Unlike physical libraries that can burn, rot, or be destroyed, a digital system with distributed storage can preserve writings, images, music, and all forms of recorded knowledge indefinitely. The system must be built to survive catastrophic events—wars, natural disasters, political upheaval—by being distributed across multiple secure locations, potentially including underground vaults and space.

This is not merely a technical feature but a moral imperative. Every time a library burns, humanity loses irreplaceable knowledge. Every time a book goes out of print, access is lost. The digital realm offers the first real opportunity to stop this attrition of the heritage.

### User Guarantee

What is published will survive. Content committed to the system becomes part of a permanent archive that can outlast any individual institution, government, or era.

### Principle Served

**Heritage Preservation**: Human knowledge should not be subject to loss through accident, decay, or deliberate destruction. The accumulated wisdom, art, and literature of civilization deserves a repository that can survive anything.

This addresses a fundamental asymmetry in history: it's trivially easy to destroy knowledge (a single fire can end centuries of work) but impossibly hard to recreate it. Xanadu shifts that balance by making preservation the default rather than the exception.

### How Users Experience It

- Content they publish enters a permanent record
- Old versions remain accessible indefinitely
- Works don't disappear when publishers fail or governments change
- The system can be replicated to multiple secure locations
- Future generations will have access to what was written today
