# Copyright

Source: Literary Machines, 2/41-2/45 (pages 121-125)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Nelson proposes an "Internal Copyright Convention" - a contractual agreement among all participants in the network that bypasses traditional copyright's complications. Instead of navigating the maze of permissions, licenses, and legal uncertainties that plague quotation in the paper world, everyone in the Xanadu network agrees to the same rules when they join.

The key insight: traditional copyright creates friction around quotation because permission must be sought, negotiated, and tracked. But in a system with automatic micropayment, the economic problem (compensating creators) is already solved. So the legal problem can be solved by contract: you agree to let anyone quote you, and in return everyone agrees to let you quote them, with all payments handled automatically.

This is not abolishing copyright - it's creating a parallel system where copyright's goals (fair compensation for creators) are achieved through technical means (automatic royalty on every byte) rather than legal enforcement.

### User Guarantee

When you participate in the network:
- You can quote any published content without asking permission (already contractually granted)
- You receive automatic royalty when your content is transmitted
- You cannot prevent others from linking to your published work
- You cannot prevent others from quoting your work (via windowing)
- You get paid the same way whether quoted directly or linked-to

The tradeoff is explicit: you give up control over how your work is used, but you never give up compensation for its use.

### Principle Served

**Fair compensation without permission friction.** The problem with traditional copyright is that it conflates two goals: (1) compensating creators and (2) controlling use. Nelson argues these can be separated. If compensation is automatic and guaranteed, then control over use becomes unnecessary - even harmful, since it creates friction that prevents legitimate quotation and interconnection.

The deeper principle: literature should be freely interconnectable. If every quotation requires permission, the web of connections that makes hypertext valuable cannot form. The Internal Copyright Convention removes this barrier while preserving the economic rights that make creation sustainable.

### How Users Experience It

**As an author:**
- You publish once, agree to the terms once
- Anyone can quote you, but you always get paid
- You don't manage permissions, track licenses, or chase infringers
- Your income is proportional to actual use, not negotiating skill

**As a reader/quoter:**
- Quote anything published without asking
- The system handles payment automatically
- No risk of infringement lawsuits (use is pre-authorized)
- Build compound documents freely from existing materials

**The difference from traditional publishing:**
- Paper: permission precedes quotation (friction)
- Xanadu: payment accompanies quotation (frictionless)

### The Contract

Nelson envisions participants signing an actual contract - something "very like a credit-card triplicate slip" - agreeing to the network's copyright convention. This creates a private legal framework parallel to statutory copyright law.

Key provisions implied by the text:
1. I agree that anyone may link and window to my published documents
2. I relinquish the right to control links into my work
3. I accept automatic royalty as my sole compensation
4. I agree to these same rules applying to my use of others' work

### Nelson's Words

On the internal convention:

> "To bypass some legal problems, we foresee establishing copyright convention internal to the network and contractually agreed upon by all participants. To wit, if you publish a thing through the network, you have to agree to the same rules as everybody else-- which are intended to create a fair balance of incentives."

On automatic permission through payment:

> "Since the copyright holder gets an automatic royalty, anything may be quoted without further permission. That is, permission has already been granted: for part of the publication contract is the provision, 'I agree that anyone may link and window to my document.'"

On relinquishing link control:

> "Accessibility and free linking make a two-sided coin. On the one hand, each user is free to link to anything privately or publicly. By the same token, each author of a published work is relinquishing the right to control links into that work. This relinquishment must also be part of the publishing contract."

On creating from existing work:

> "Thus users may create new published documents out of old ones indefinitely, making whatever changes seem appropriate-- without damaging the originals. This is done by inclusion links."

On automatic royalty mechanics:

> "In our planned service, there is a royalty on every byte transmitted. This is paid automatically by the user to the owner every time a fragment is summoned, as part of the proportional use of byte delivery."
