# Publishing

Source: Literary Machines, Chapter 2 (2/41-2/52)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Publishing in Xanadu is the act of making your document universally available. A document is either **private** (accessible only to you and your designees) or **published** (accessible to everyone in the network). Publication is not just about visibility - it's a social and economic act that creates binding agreements: you agree to let anyone link to your work, and the system agrees to pay you royalties when your content is used.

Nelson frames this as extending the "true structure of text" into a universal system. The key insight: if we already have a hyperfile system with links and compound documents, why not make it a publishing system where *any user* can read, link to, and quote from *anything published*?

### User Guarantee

When you publish a document:
- It becomes permanently available to everyone
- You cannot easily withdraw it (like a printed book)
- Anyone can link to it without your permission
- Anyone can quote from it (via windows) without separate permission
- You receive automatic royalties proportional to how much of your content is transmitted

When you keep a document private:
- Only you and your designees can access it
- You can modify or withdraw it at any time
- It can still link to and window public documents

### Principle Served

**Universal literature with fair compensation.** Traditional publishing created artificial scarcity - only some works got published, and the system favored publishers over authors. Nelson envisions a system where *anyone can publish instantly*, reaching everyone, while getting paid fairly through automatic micropayments.

The problem with paper publishing:
- High barriers to publication (printers, warehouses, distribution)
- Limited selection (publishers filter what reaches readers)
- Quotation requires permission (creating friction)

Xanadu's solution:
- Instant publication with minimal cost (just disk rental)
- Universal accessibility (anything published, anyone can read)
- Automatic quotation rights with automatic payment

### How Users Experience It

**Publishing:**
- Create your document privately (write, edit, perfect it)
- When ready, perform a deliberate "act of publication" (a ceremony, not just a button)
- Your document enters the permanent public record
- Anyone can find it, read it, link to it, quote from it
- Royalties flow automatically when your content is transmitted

**Reading and quoting:**
- Access any published document instantly
- Make links from your documents to any published content
- Quote by windowing - the original owner gets paid automatically
- No need to ask permission for quotation (already granted by publication contract)

**The perpetual nature:**
- Published documents stay published (like books in libraries)
- Authors may publish corrections/updates as new versions
- Old versions remain accessible (others may have linked to them)
- Links to old versions can "follow through" to new versions if the reader prefers

### The Act of Publication

Publication is a solemn event, not casual:

> "Because publication is an important act, both for authors and readers, we make publication a solemn event, to be undertaken cautiously. It could be effected merely by a 'publish' button on the user's console-- but the dangers of rash publication to an individual's reputation, legal liabilities and career could be great."

Nelson envisions signing a contract - perhaps on "something very like a credit-card triplicate slip" - committing to the publication and its terms.

### No Control Over In-Links

A key tradeoff of publication:

> "Accessibility and free linking make a two-sided coin. On the one hand, each user is free to link to anything privately or publicly. By the same token, each author of a published work is relinquishing the right to control links into that work. This relinquishment must also be part of the publishing contract."

You can't prevent others from linking to your published work. This enables the "socially self-constructing" literature Nelson envisions.

### The Royalty Model

Payment happens automatically:

> "In our planned service, there is a royalty on every byte transmitted. This is paid automatically by the user to the owner every time a fragment is summoned, as part of the proportional use of byte delivery."

Royalties are:
- Per-byte (proportional to actual content delivered)
- Automatic (built into the system, not negotiated)
- Split fairly (when quoting, original owner and quoter each get their share)
- Standardized (everyone uses the same rates, part of the contract)

Example: If someone quotes your work in their document, when that quote is transmitted to a reader:
- You get royalty for your words
- The quoter gets royalty for their arrangement/commentary
- Proportional to who wrote what

### Privashing

An alternative to full publication:

> "An author who wishes to render his work universally available, but wishes also to retain the right to withdraw it at any time, has a simple means for so doing. The author simply designates his or her document as a private document with unrestricted distribution."

Privashing = Private + Publishing-like access:
- Anyone can access it
- But the owner can withdraw it at any time
- No royalty is received (incentive to publish formally)
- Anyone who linked to it has no recourse if you withdraw

### What Copies Lose

Nelson emphasizes that copying is self-defeating:

> "Note also that he or she who makes a paper copy or disk is losing all dynamic link connections, and is left with the inert, non-interactive copy. And that will be a considerable deprivation in the world we are talking about."

The metaphor of water:

> "Remember the analogy between text and water. Water flows freely, ice does not. The free-flowing, live documents on the network are subject to constant new use and linkage, and those new links continually become interactively available. Any detached copy someone keeps is frozen and dead, lacking access to the new linkage."

### Nelson's Words

On the vision:

> "Our system is not intended just as a private facility, but also as a method of publication. Thus a carefully designed system of publication, including conventions for copyright and royalty-- surprisingly like that of paper publishing-- has been worked out."

On universal quotation:

> "Since the copyright holder gets an automatic royalty, anything may be quoted without further permission. That is, permission has already been granted: for part of the publication contract is the provision, 'I agree that anyone may link and window to my document.'"

On the power of compound documents:

> "This is a radical and daring idea: a new form of reading and writing, in a way just like the old (but faster), with quotations and marginalia and citations. Yet it will also be socially self-constructing into a vast new traversable framework, a new literature."

On permanence:

> "It is in the common interest that a thing once published stay published, as in the world of paper. Other readers and users will come to depend on its accessibility. Consequently its author may not withdraw it except by lengthy due process."

On modified versions:

> "Thus users may create new published documents out of old ones indefinitely, making whatever changes seem appropriate-- without damaging the originals. This is done by inclusion links."

On the role of publishers:

> "Even if (as we believe) compound hypertext is the writing of the future, and a system like ours is the printing press of the future, the publisher of the future can do all these things in exactly the same way. Except now there is no 'printing and warehousing,' but a certain required minimum disk rental. Thus a 'publisher' is someone who pays for the rapid accessibility of materials and benefits from their use along with the author."

On sieving in-links:

> "The reader should be able to ask, for a given document or place in the document, 'What connects here from other documents?'-- and be shown all these outside conections without appreciable delay."

On legitimate use:

> "There is no way whatever to ascertain or control what happens at the users' terminals. Therefore perforce all use whatever is legitimate, and anyone who plans to be vulnerable to 'misuse,' whatever he or she thinks that may be, had better keep his or her stuff off the system."
