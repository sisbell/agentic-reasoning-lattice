# The Purpose of Xanadu

Source: Literary Machines, synthesized from 1/2-1/4, 2/4-2/11, 2/59, 4/4

## The Fundamental Problem

Nelson identifies a profound failure in how we handle written knowledge:

> "A literature is a system of interconnected writings. We do not offer this as our definition, but as a discovered fact. And almost all writing is part of some literature." (2/9)

But existing systems **destroy these connections**:

> "Links between documents help us follow the connections... But they are there, the documents do not present them." (2/11)

Traditional storage (files, databases, the web) treats documents as **isolated objects**. This is a category error. Writing is inherently interconnected - quotations, citations, responses, influences - but our systems hide these connections instead of preserving them.

---

## What Xanadu Solves That Others Cannot

### 1. The Permanence Problem

> "There is no predicting the use future people will make of what is written. Any summary we write today embodies a particular view, the perspective of a particular time. We cannot know how documents will be seen in the future. We must assume there will never be a final and definitive view of anything." (2/11)

**Xanadu guarantee:** "Any address of any document in an ever-growing network may be specified by a permanent tumbler address." (4/19) Once published, content stays published. Addresses are permanent.

### 2. The Attribution Problem

> "You always know where you are, and can at once ascertain the home document of any specific word or character." (2/40)

**Xanadu guarantee:** Attribution is structural. Every byte traces to its origin. Transclusion (inclusion by reference) maintains the connection permanently.

### 3. The Connection Problem

> "In any ongoing literature, there is perpetual interpretation and reinterpretation, and links between documents help us follow the connections." (2/10)

**Xanadu guarantee:** Links are bidirectional in discovery. Links survive editing. Links are owned separately from the documents they connect.

### 4. The Version Problem

> "The file management system we are talking about automatically keeps track of the changes and the pieces, so that when you ask for a given part of a given version at a given time, it comes to your screen." (2/14)

**Xanadu guarantee:** All versions persist. You can always go back. New versions reference old content without copying or destroying it.

### 5. The Quotation Problem

> "Since the copyright holder gets an automatic royalty, anything may be quoted without further permission." (2/45)

**Xanadu guarantee:** Permission is pre-granted through publication contract. Royalties flow automatically with content delivery.

## The Core Invariant: Literature as System

Nelson's insight is that **literature is debugged**:

> "In the evolution of the printed word, the mechanisms of quotation, citation and bibliography have been carefully adjusted in an evolving process." (2/10)

The purpose of Xanadu is to **preserve and expose the structure of literature** - the web of connections that makes knowledge self-correcting.

---

## The Contractual Foundation

Nelson describes Xanadu as having three structures:

> "a rational conceptual structure, plus a technical structure which makes it possible, and a contractual structure which makes it possible for people to use it confidently." (4/4)

The contractual structure is essential - not just policy but system design:

> "Because the conceptual structure expects participants to behave in certain ways, these are embraced in the contract offered to users. These provisions are necessary for the orderly and confident use of published material by many people." (4/4)

This is why certain guarantees are **requirements**, not features:
- Published content cannot be withdrawn (others have linked to it)
- Authors must permit quotation (that's the publication contract)
- The system must not surveil users (freedom requires it)

---

## Nelson's Words: The Essential Vision

On purpose:
> "This is the design of the system we propose for tomorrow's literature, tomorrow's office, and for the archives of our thought, controversy, art and architecture." (2/4)

On simplicity:
> "The starting point in designing a computer system must be the creation of the conceptual and psychological environment--the seeming of the system--what I and my associates call the virtuality. You begin by designing a conceptual structure and how it should feel, then work back into the mechanics." (1/3)

On the stakes:
> "This is a libertarian system: restrict it, and all will lose." (2/59)

---

## Summary: The Purpose

**Xanadu exists to preserve the structure of literature.**

Literature is interconnected by nature. Traditional systems destroy these connections through copying, moving, deleting, and breaking links. Xanadu maintains:

1. **Permanence** - addresses never break
2. **Attribution** - origins always traceable
3. **Connections** - links always work
4. **History** - versions always accessible
5. **Rights** - royalties always flow

This is not a feature list but a **conservation law**: the interconnected structure of literature must be preserved, not destroyed, by the system that stores it.
