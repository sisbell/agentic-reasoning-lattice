# Anonymous Publication and Royalties

Source: Literary Machines, 2/59-2/60 (pages 139-140), 5/11-5/13 (pages 257-259)

## What It Means

Nelson explicitly supports "John Doe publication" - anonymous publication by "walk-in and transient users" who do not identify themselves. This is a deliberate feature rooted in his libertarian vision of the system as "the printing press of the future."

However, Nelson does NOT specify what happens to royalties when the publisher is anonymous. This creates a significant gap between two explicitly stated principles:

1. **ROYALTY ON EVERY BYTE**: "There is a royalty on every byte transmitted. This is paid automatically by the user to the owner every time a fragment is summoned." (5/12)

2. **ANONYMOUS PUBLICATION ALLOWED**: "There is no reason that anonymous publication by walk-in and transient users of this system should not be allowed. We may call this 'John Doe publication.'" (2/60)

If John Doe publishes content and readers pay per-byte royalties, the money must go SOMEWHERE - but Nelson never says where.

## What IS Specified

**Royalty Mechanics (5/12-5/13):**
- Surcharge per byte: $.000001/byte
- "This accumulates to the cash register of the given document"
- When charges incurred exceed royalty author's reserve, "the remainder becomes the author's profit"

**Author's Fund (5/12):**
- "When bytes are taken from an unpublished document (one in the public domain), this surcharge is added to the Author's Fund"
- The Author's Fund is "an escrow account whose purpose is the charitable funding of worthy causes within the network"
- Includes "subsidies to struggling writers and artists"

**Anonymous Publication (2/60):**
- "John Doe publication" explicitly allowed
- Walk-in, transient users need not identify themselves
- Anonymous content is "more sensitive to challenge" through PEREMPTORY CHALLENGE

## What Is NOT Specified

Nelson does NOT address:

1. **Where royalties go for anonymous publishers** - The cash register accumulates charges, but if the owner is John Doe, who receives the profit?

2. **Escrow for anonymous royalties** - Is there a mechanism to hold royalties pending identity claim?

3. **Claiming accumulated earnings** - Can a John Doe later prove authorship and collect?

4. **Timeout for unclaimed royalties** - Do unclaimed royalties eventually go to the Author's Fund?

5. **Payment vs. storage contradiction** - Anonymous publication implies no account, but storage requires ongoing payment

## The Storage Paradox

Nelson's model requires **ongoing storage rental** paid by the owner (page 4/5, 5/11). But if the publisher is anonymous (no account, no identity), who pays for storage?

Possible interpretations:
- Anonymous publication requires **prepaid storage** (walk-in pays upfront)
- Anonymous content becomes **dark** when prepayment expires
- Anonymous is **pseudonymous** (account exists but real identity hidden)

Nelson does not clarify which interpretation is correct.

## Interaction with Peremptory Challenge

The one scenario Nelson DOES address is removal, not payment:

> "Consider libelous or uncomplimentary John Doe graffiti defaming specific individuals. If John Doe is not available, the affected individual must be able to effect removal of the materials by peremptory challenge." (2/60)

This creates an interesting asymmetry:
- **Defamation**: Can be removed because John Doe won't defend
- **Royalties**: Presumably still accrue even without an identified owner

## Principle Served

**Freedom of expression without mandatory identity.** Nelson's libertarian vision requires that anonymity be possible. The printing press analogy applies: just as one can distribute anonymous pamphlets, one can publish anonymously on the network.

The unresolved economic questions are either:
- An oversight in the specification
- Intentionally left to implementation
- Assumed to be rare enough to handle ad hoc

## Nelson's Words

On anonymous publication:
> "Normally publication will be by accountable individuals and companies with known residence or place of business. Still, there is no reason that anonymous publication by walk-in and transient users of this system should not be allowed. We may call this 'John Doe publication.'"

On challenging anonymous content:
> "Consider libelous or uncomplimentary John Doe graffiti defaming specific individuals. If John Doe is not available, the affected individual must be able to effect removal of the materials by peremptory challenge."

On the libertarian nature:
> "There is no thinking out all these eventualities. But the system we propose is a libertarian system: restrict it, and all will lose."

On royalty flow:
> "This accumulates to the cash register of the given document; when the network charges incurred by that document have been offset, the remainder becomes the author's profit."

On the Author's Fund:
> "When bytes are taken from an unpublished document (one in the public domain), this surcharge is added to the Author's Fund, an escrow account whose purpose is the charitable funding of worthy causes within the network. These causes can include the costs of input and proofreading of non-owned documents, subsidies to struggling writers and artists, and other worthy purposes."

## User Guarantee

NONE SPECIFIED for anonymous royalties. This is a gap in Nelson's specification.

What IS guaranteed:
- Anonymous publication is permitted ("John Doe publication")
- Anonymous content can be removed via peremptory challenge if defamatory
- Royalties are collected on every byte delivered
- Author's Fund exists for public domain content

What is NOT guaranteed:
- Anonymous publishers will receive royalties
- Accumulated royalties will be held for future claim
- Any specific disposition of unclaimed royalties
