**T2 (IntrinsicComparison).** The order relation T1 is computable from the two tumblers alone, without consulting any external data structure. The number of component pairs the comparison examines is bounded by both `#a` and `#b` ‚Äî at most `#a` pairs and at most `#b` pairs.

*Proof.* We must establish two claims: (1) the comparison requires no data beyond the components and lengths of the two tumblers, and (2) the number of component pairs examined is at most `#a` and at most `#b`.

Let `a = a‚ÇÅ. ... .a‚Çò` and `b = b‚ÇÅ. ... .b‚Çô`. The definition of `<` in T1 asks for the existence of a witness position `k ‚â• 1` satisfying two conditions: agreement at all positions before `k`, and either a component divergence at `k` or prefix exhaustion at `k`. We trace exactly what data this examination consults and how many comparisons it performs.

The comparison proceeds by scanning positions `i = 1, 2, ...` and comparing the pair `(a·µ¢, b·µ¢)` at each position. Two outcomes are possible.

*Case 1: divergence at some position `k` with `k ‚â§ m ‚àß k ‚â§ n`.* The scan finds `a‚Çñ ‚âÝ b‚Çñ` after verifying `a·µ¢ = b·µ¢` for all `1 ‚â§ i < k`. Exactly `k` component pairs are examined. The case hypothesis's first conjunct `k ‚â§ m` gives `k ‚â§ #a`, and its second conjunct `k ‚â§ n` gives `k ‚â§ #b`, so the count `k` is bounded by both `#a` and `#b`. NAT-order's trichotomy at the component pair `(a‚Çñ, b‚Çñ)` resolves `a‚Çñ ‚âÝ b‚Çñ` into the two-way split `a‚Çñ < b‚Çñ ‚à® b‚Çñ < a‚Çñ` ‚Äî its remaining disjunct `a‚Çñ = b‚Çñ` is excluded by the case hypothesis, and the exactly-one clause guarantees one of the strict comparisons holds. Whichever holds decides the ordering via T1 case (i): `a‚Çñ < b‚Çñ` yields `a < b`, and `b‚Çñ < a‚Çñ` yields `b < a`. Both outcomes are comparisons of two natural numbers intrinsic to the tumblers.

*Case 2: no divergence within the shared range.* The scan exhausts every position `i` with `1 ‚â§ i ‚àß i ‚â§ m ‚àß i ‚â§ n` ‚Äî the shared range ‚Äî without finding `a·µ¢ ‚âÝ b·µ¢`. The count of component pairs examined and the ordering decision are together determined by NAT-order's trichotomy at the length pair `(m, n)`, which partitions the outcome exhaustively into three branches. *Sub-case `m < n`.* The shared range is exactly `{i : 1 ‚â§ i ‚â§ m}` ‚Äî every position of `a`, each of which satisfies `i ‚â§ n` via the case hypothesis ‚Äî so exactly `m` component pairs are examined; that count satisfies `m ‚â§ m` reflexively and `m ‚â§ n` from the case hypothesis's strict comparison read as the strict disjunct of `‚â§`. Then `a` is a proper prefix of `b` and T1 case (ii) gives `a < b`. *Sub-case `n < m`.* Symmetric: the shared range covers all of `b`'s positions, exactly `n` component pairs are examined, satisfying `n ‚â§ m` (from `n < m`) and `n ‚â§ n` reflexively, and T1 case (ii) with roles swapped gives `b < a`. *Sub-case `m = n`.* The shared range covers every position of either tumbler, so exactly `m` component pairs are examined; that count satisfies `m ‚â§ m` reflexively and `m ‚â§ n` by substitution of `m = n`. Componentwise agreement on the shared range together with `m = n` gives `a = b` by T3. In every sub-case the count of pairs examined is at most `m` and at most `n`. The exhaustiveness of this three-way partition ‚Äî the "exactly one" clause of NAT-order's trichotomy ‚Äî is what underwrites postcondition (a) for this branch: some ordering among `a` and `b` is always decided, because no fourth outcome is possible for the length pair `(m, n)`.

In both cases, the number of component pairs examined is at most `m` and at most `n` ‚Äî equivalently, at most `#a` and at most `#b` ‚Äî establishing claim (2).

For claim (1), we observe that every value consulted ‚Äî the components `a·µ¢` and `b·µ¢` at each position, and the lengths `m` and `n` ‚Äî belongs to the two tumblers themselves. The definition of T1 is expressed entirely in terms of these values. No tree structure, no index, no auxiliary mapping, and no external state participates in the decision. ‚àé

The importance of T2 is operational: span containment tests, link search, and index traversal all reduce to tumbler comparison. If comparison required a lookup, these operations would depend on auxiliary state, and the system's decentralization guarantee would collapse ‚Äî one could not determine whether an address falls within a span without access to the index that manages that span.

Gregory's implementation confirms T2. The comparison function `tumblercmp` delegates to `abscmp`, which performs a purely positional comparison: exponent first (a proxy for the number of leading zeros), then lexicographic mantissa slot-by-slot. No tree structure, no index, no external state is consulted.
