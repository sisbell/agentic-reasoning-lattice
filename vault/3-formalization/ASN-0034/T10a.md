**T10a (AllocatorDiscipline).** The root allocator begins from a base address satisfying T4. Each allocator produces its sibling outputs exclusively by repeated application of `inc(·, 0)` — shallow increment at the last significant position. To spawn a child allocator, the parent performs one `inc(·, k')` with `k' ∈ {1, 2}`, subject to the runtime precondition — imposed by the discipline and checked before each spawn — that TA5a's zero-count bound holds on the chosen domain element `t`: `zeros(t) ≤ 3` when `k' = 1`, `zeros(t) ≤ 2` when `k' = 2`, where `zeros(·)` is the zero-count function defined in T4. The axiom borrows `zeros(·)` from T4 as a precondition symbol whose meaning the axiom itself relies on to be well-formed, and from TA5a it adopts the preservation bounds `zeros(t) ≤ 3` (for `k' = 1`) and `zeros(t) ≤ 2` (for `k' = 2`) as runtime preconditions on child-spawning; a spawning event failing either bound is not admissible under the discipline. The `inc(·, k')` thus chosen establishes the child's prefix, then further allocation is delegated to the child. Each pair `(t, k')` — a domain element `t` of the parent together with a spawning parameter `k' ∈ {1, 2}` — produces at most one child-spawning event; the parent may not invoke `inc(t, k')` for the same `t` and `k'` in two distinct spawning operations. The parent's own sibling stream resumes with `inc(·, 0)`.

T10a constrains what would otherwise be an unregulated choice. Without it, an allocator could intermix shallow and deep increments, producing outputs of varying lengths whose prefix relationships would be uncontrolled. The child-spawning operation — a single deep increment with `k' ∈ {1, 2}` — establishes a new prefix at a deeper level, from which a new allocator continues with its own `inc(·, 0)` stream. The restriction to `k' ∈ {1, 2}` with the zero-count conditions of TA5a ensures that child-spawning preserves T4; unrestricted `k' ≥ 3` would introduce adjacent zeros, violating T4's field-segment constraint (TA5a, case `k ≥ 3`).

*Definitions.* The consequences below quantify over distinct allocators, ancestor-descendant pairs, and parent-child relationships, so before giving an allocator's observable output we must first say what an allocator *is* as an object, when two allocators are considered equal, and how the tree relations are derived. We formalize allocators as nodes of a tree induced by the axiom's spawning rule, then build the per-allocator domain and the `same_allocator` predicate on top of that structure.

*Allocator tree.* The *allocator tree* 𝒯 consists of a distinguished *root allocator* together with every allocator reachable by finite iteration of the child-spawning rule. Each non-root allocator `A` carries a *spawning triple* `(parent(A), spawnPt(A), spawnParam(A))`, where `parent(A) ∈ 𝒯` is the allocator that spawned `A`, `spawnPt(A) ∈ dom(parent(A))` is the parent domain element from which `A` was spawned, and `spawnParam(A) ∈ {1, 2}` is the spawning parameter. `A`'s *base address* is then `inc(spawnPt(A), spawnParam(A))`, determined from the triple by TA5, with `A`'s subsequent outputs produced by iterated `inc(·, 0)`. The root carries no spawning triple; its base address is the T4-valid address fixed by the initialization constraint stated in the axiom. The tree is defined by induction on depth: level 0 is the singleton `{root}`, and level `d + 1` consists of every allocator whose spawning triple's parent lies at level `d` — a well-founded construction because `dom(parent(A))` is already defined at the earlier level.

*Identity and distinctness.* Two allocators `X` and `Y` are *equal* — written `X = Y` — iff either both are the root, or both are non-root with `parent(X) = parent(Y)` (allocator equality at lesser depth, defined inductively), `spawnPt(X) = spawnPt(Y)` (equality of tumblers), and `spawnParam(X) = spawnParam(Y)` (equality of spawning parameters). Equivalently, `X = Y` iff the finite sequence of spawning triples on the root-path to each is identical. Two allocators are *distinct* iff `X ≠ Y`. The axiom's at-most-once child-spawning constraint — "each pair `(t, k')` yields at most one child-spawning event" at a given parent — is the dynamic counterpart of this identity criterion: it forbids a single parent from producing two distinct children that share the same `(t, k')`, so the spawning-triple identification over 𝒯 does not over-count children of any parent. Under this identity criterion, "distinct allocators" in T10a.6 and GlobalUniqueness, and "allocators not in an ancestor-descendant relationship" in T10a.5, each denote well-defined relations on 𝒯.

*Derived tree relations.* `Y` is a *child* of `X` iff `parent(Y) = X`. The *depth* function `depth : 𝒯 → ℕ` is defined by `depth(root) = 0` and `depth(Y) = depth(parent(Y)) + 1` for non-root `Y`; the finite length of every root-path makes this well-defined. `X` is an *ancestor* of `Y` iff `X = Y` or `X` is an ancestor of `parent(Y)` — equivalently, iff a finite chain `X = A₀, A₁, …, Aₙ = Y` exists with `parent(Aᵢ) = Aᵢ₋₁` for `1 ≤ i ≤ n`. `Y` is a *descendant* of `X` iff `X` is an ancestor of `Y`. `X` and `Y` are *in an ancestor-descendant relationship* iff one is an ancestor of the other; otherwise they are *not in an ancestor-descendant relationship*. These derived relations fix the denotation of "parent allocator", "param-1 child", and "param-2 child" used in PartitionMonotonicity: a param-1 child of `X` is a child `C` of `X` with `spawnParam(C) = 1`, and a param-2 child is one with `spawnParam(C) = 2`. The prefix *param-* names the spawn parameter and is disjoint from the tree-level `depth` function defined above: the depth of any child `C` of `X` is `depth(X) + 1` regardless of whether `C` is a param-1 or a param-2 child.

*Domain.* For each allocator `A` with base address `t₀` — the root's T4-valid initialization when `A` is the root, or `t₀ = inc(spawnPt(A), spawnParam(A))` when `A` is non-root — its *domain* is the set

  `dom(A) = {tₙ : n ≥ 0}` where `tₙ₊₁ = inc(tₙ, 0)`,

the base address together with every iterated sibling output; the axiom's restriction of sibling production to `inc(·, 0)` makes this enumeration well-defined, and `inc` itself is supplied by TA5. Child-spawning outputs are excluded from the parent's domain by construction: when `A` performs `inc(t, k')` with `k' > 0`, the resulting address `c₀ = inc(t, k')` is not an element of `dom(A)` but serves as the base of the child allocator `C` with `parent(C) = A`, `spawnPt(C) = t`, `spawnParam(C) = k'`, becoming the initial element of `dom(C)`. We further define the predicate

  `same_allocator(a, b) ≡ ∃A ∈ 𝒯 : a ∈ dom(A) ∧ b ∈ dom(A)`,

asserting that two addresses both lie in the domain of a single allocator; the existential quantifier now ranges over the well-defined tree 𝒯. All definitions are well-founded relative to T10a's own dependencies: the tree is induced inductively by the axiom's spawning rule, the domain construction uses `inc` from TA5, and the base-address and sibling-production restrictions are supplied by the axiom itself.

*Justification.* T10a is a design axiom — it constrains allocator behavior rather than following from prior properties. The consequences below rest on eight foundations: TA5, which defines `inc` and supplies the length postconditions `#inc(t, 0) = #t` (TA5(c)) and `#inc(t, k) = #t + k` for `k > 0` (TA5(d)), together with the strict monotonicity `inc(t, 0) > t` (TA5(a)); TA5a (IncrementPreservesT4), which establishes that `inc(t, k)` preserves T4 iff `k = 0`, or `k = 1 ∧ zeros(t) ≤ 3`, or `k = 2 ∧ zeros(t) ≤ 2`, and fails for `k ≥ 3`; TA5-SigValid (SigOnValidAddresses), which fixes `sig(t) = #t` for every T4-valid tumbler and thereby identifies the terminal component as the single position advanced by `inc(·, 0)`, feeding the inductive step of Consequence 5 (where the divergence position is propagated by distinguishing interior from sig-modified positions) and the zero-count preservation argument of Consequence 8 (where the advanced position is located at the non-zero terminal component); T4 (HierarchicalParsing), whose field-segment constraint is the invariant that the discipline must preserve; T3 (CanonicalRepresentation), whose component-wise equality criterion, read contrapositively, guarantees that two distinct tumblers of equal length differ at some position — the divergence supplying the branching-point argument in the base case of Consequence 5 when the lowest common ancestor spawns its two differing children from distinct parent tumblers; T10 (PartitionIndependence), whose precondition — non-nesting prefixes — is the guarantee that T10a's discipline establishes for all non-ancestor-descendant allocator pairs (ancestor-descendant pairs inherently nest, as shown in Consequence 5); Prefix (PrefixRelation), from which it follows that tumblers of equal length are prefix-related only if identical, and whose definition underwrites the necessity argument (T10a-N); and T1 (LexicographicOrder), whose irreflexivity (T1 (a)) and transitivity (T1 (c)) lift the per-step monotonicity of `inc(·, 0)` to the strict increase of the entire enumeration chain (Consequence 7). We justify the constraint by establishing seven consequences on which the coordination-free uniqueness guarantees depend, then proving that the sibling restriction is necessary for prefix-incomparability.

**Consequence 1: Uniform sibling length.** Since T10a restricts sibling production to `inc(·, 0)`, and TA5(c) guarantees `#inc(t, 0) = #t`, every sibling output has the same length as the allocator's base address (T10a.1).

**Consequence 2: Non-nesting sibling prefixes.** By T10a.1, all siblings have equal length; tumblers of equal length are prefix-related only if identical (Prefix), so distinct siblings are prefix-incomparable (T10a.2).

**Consequence 3: Length separation between parent and child domains.** When the parent performs `inc(·, k')` with `k' ∈ {1, 2}`, TA5(d) gives `#inc(t, k') = #t + k'`, so the child's base address has length strictly exceeding any parent sibling. All child siblings then preserve this length by T10a.1, and across `d` nesting levels the separation is additive (T10a.3).

**Consequence 4: T4 preservation under the discipline.** The proof proceeds by induction on the allocator tree. *Base:* the root allocator's base address satisfies T4 by the initialization constraint. *Step:* given an allocator whose base address satisfies T4, TA5a guarantees that `inc(·, 0)` unconditionally preserves T4 (case `k = 0`), and the axiom restricts child-spawning to `k' ∈ {1, 2}` with the zero-count bounds of TA5a, so every child-spawning output `inc(t, k')` satisfies T4 — and this output serves as the child allocator's base, closing the induction. Every output of a conforming allocator therefore satisfies T4. Properties T4a–T4c and all downstream consumers of T4 are guaranteed their precondition by the discipline itself (T10a.4).

**Consequence 5: Cross-allocator prefix-incomparability.** Consequences 1–2 guarantee non-nesting among siblings of a single allocator; Consequence 3 separates parent from child by length. For ancestor-descendant allocator pairs, prefix-nesting holds by construction: when a parent spawns a child from output `tᵢ` via `inc(tᵢ, k')` with `k' > 0`, TA5(b) gives agreement on all positions `1..#tᵢ` and TA5(d) gives `#inc(tᵢ, k') > #tᵢ`, so `tᵢ ≼ inc(tᵢ, k')` — the spawning output is a proper prefix of the child's base address, and positional inheritance propagates this through the child's entire subtree. T10's non-nesting precondition therefore does not hold for such pairs, nor is it needed: their outputs have distinct lengths by T10a.3 and hence are distinct. For the remaining case — allocators not in an ancestor-descendant relationship — T10's precondition must be established. We prove that every such pair produces prefix-incomparable outputs (T10a.5).

Let X and Y be allocators not in an ancestor-descendant relationship. They share a lowest common ancestor P in the allocator tree; let Cₓ be the child of P on the path to X and Cᵧ the child on the path to Y. Were Cₓ = Cᵧ, that child would be a lower common ancestor, contradicting minimality, so Cₓ ≠ Cᵧ. Write m for the common length of P's domain elements (T10a.1). P spawns Cₓ from `(tₓ, k'ₓ)` and Cᵧ from `(tᵧ, k'ᵧ)` with `k'ₓ, k'ᵧ ∈ {1, 2}`, and the at-most-once constraint requires distinct spawning pairs: `(tₓ, k'ₓ) ≠ (tᵧ, k'ᵧ)`. Let `bₓ = inc(tₓ, k'ₓ)` and `bᵧ = inc(tᵧ, k'ᵧ)`.

*Base case: the branching point produces a divergence position.* Since `(tₓ, k'ₓ) ≠ (tᵧ, k'ᵧ)`, either `tₓ ≠ tᵧ` or `k'ₓ ≠ k'ᵧ`. If `tₓ ≠ tᵧ`: both are domain elements of P with `#tₓ = #tᵧ = m` (T10a.1), so by T3 they differ at some position `j ≤ m`; TA5(b) for `k > 0` preserves these positions, giving `bₓ[j] = tₓ[j] ≠ tᵧ[j] = bᵧ[j]`, with `j < #bₓ` and `j < #bᵧ`. If `tₓ = tᵧ` and `k'ₓ ≠ k'ᵧ`: one of `{k'ₓ, k'ᵧ}` is 1 and the other 2; take `k'ₓ = 1` and `k'ᵧ = 2` without loss of generality. TA5(d) places a 1 at position `m + 1` of `bₓ` (the single appended position) and a 0 at position `m + 1` of `bᵧ` (the zero-valued separator preceding the final 1), so `j = m + 1` with `bₓ[j] = 1 ≠ 0 = bᵧ[j]` and `j = min(#bₓ, #bᵧ)`. In both cases a position `j ≤ min(#bₓ, #bᵧ)` exists at which `bₓ[j] ≠ bᵧ[j]`.

*Inductive step: divergence propagates through the allocator tree.* We show by induction on depth that every output in the subtree rooted at Cₓ preserves the value `bₓ[j]` at position j — or, in the second case, preserves a strictly positive value there — and symmetrically for Cᵧ. Within any allocator A having base `b_A` satisfying T4, the `inc(·, 0)` chain enumerating `dom(A)` modifies only position `sig(b_A) = #b_A`: TA5(b) for `k = 0` preserves every position other than `sig`, and TA5(c) establishes actual modification at `sig` with `t'_{sig} = t_{sig} + 1` (TA5-SigValid and T10a.4 give `sig(b_A) = #b_A`). When A spawns a child from an element s of `dom(A)` via `inc(s, k'')` with `k'' > 0`, TA5(b) copies positions `1..#s` into the child's base, whose length `#s + k''` exceeds `#s` (TA5(d)), so all inherited positions are thereafter interior and fixed.

When `j < #bₓ` — the case arising from `tₓ ≠ tᵧ` — position j differs from `sig(bₓ) = #bₓ`, so every element of `dom(Cₓ)` carries `bₓ[j]` at position j. Each child inherits it via TA5(b), and at every deeper level `#b_A > #bₓ > j`, so j is never the significant position of any descendant. The value `bₓ[j]` propagates unchanged through the entire subtree; the same argument carries `bᵧ[j]` through Cᵧ's subtree.

When `j = #bₓ` — the case arising from `k'ₓ = 1`, `k'ᵧ = 2` — successive elements of `dom(Cₓ)` increment position j (since `sig(bₓ) = #bₓ = j`), producing values `1, 2, 3, …` starting from `bₓ[j] = 1` (TA5(c)). Every element of `dom(Cₓ)` therefore carries a strictly positive value at position j. When Cₓ spawns a grandchild from an element s of `dom(Cₓ)`, TA5(b) copies `s[j] ≥ 1` into the grandchild's base, whose length exceeds j; from that depth onward, position j is interior and the positive value propagates unchanged. Every output in Cₓ's subtree has a strictly positive value at position j. On the Cᵧ side, `j = m + 1 < m + 2 = #bᵧ` (since `k'ᵧ = 2`), so position j is interior to Cᵧ's base; the `inc(·, 0)` chain enumerating `dom(Cᵧ)` leaves it at `bᵧ[j] = 0`, and the same inheritance argument carries 0 through every descendant. The subtrees of Cₓ and Cᵧ carry distinct values at position j.

*Closure.* For any `x ∈ domain(X)` and `y ∈ domain(Y)`: X is Cₓ or a descendant of Cₓ, so T10a.3 (local monotonicity) gives `#x ≥ #bₓ` — with equality when X = Cₓ, by T10a.1; symmetrically `#y ≥ #bᵧ`. Since `j ≤ min(#bₓ, #bᵧ)` (established in the base case), both `#x ≥ j` and `#y ≥ j`, and `x[j] ≠ y[j]` by the inductive step. Were `x ≼ y`, Prefix would require `x[i] = y[i]` for all `i ≤ #x`, giving `x[j] = y[j]` — a contradiction. Symmetrically `y ⋠ x`. ∎

**Consequence 6: Domain disjointness.** Consequences 1, 3, and 5 together imply that distinct allocators have disjoint domains. For an ancestor–descendant pair, T10a.1 gives uniform length within each domain and T10a.3 gives a strict length inequality between ancestor and descendant streams, so no element can lie in both. For a non-ancestor–descendant pair, T10a.5 gives prefix-incomparability of all outputs, contradicted by the trivial reflexivity `t ≼ t` were `t` shared. The disjointness elevates `same_allocator(a, b)` from a bare existential to a witness-unique predicate: when `same_allocator(a, b)` holds, the witnessing allocator `A` with `a, b ∈ dom(A)` is uniquely determined by the pair `(a, b)` (T10a.6). Witness-uniqueness alone does not yet make the enumeration indices single-valued — a single allocator's chain `t₀, t₁, t₂, …` could in principle revisit the same tumbler under two indices — so the well-definedness of any predicate phrased over those indices, `allocated_before(a, b)` in T9 in particular, requires a complementary within-allocator injectivity, supplied next.

**Consequence 7: Enumeration injectivity.** Within any single allocator's chain `t₀, t₁, t₂, …`, the indexing map `n ↦ tₙ` is injective: `m ≠ n` implies `tₘ ≠ tₙ`. By TA5(a), each step produces a strictly greater tumbler, `tₙ₊₁ = inc(tₙ, 0) > tₙ`; induction on the gap using T1 (c) transitivity lifts this per-step increase to `tₘ < tₙ` for every `m < n`; T1 (a) irreflexivity then excludes `tₘ = tₙ`, yielding distinctness. Combined with Consequence 6, this secures single-valuedness of the enumeration indices `i, j` with `a = tᵢ, b = tⱼ` as functions of the pair `(a, b)` whenever `same_allocator(a, b)` holds: the witnessing allocator is unique (Consequence 6), and within it the index assigned to each element is unique (Consequence 7). Any predicate phrased over those indices — `allocated_before(a, b)` in T9 — therefore receives an unambiguous interpretation (T10a.7).

**Consequence 8: Uniform sibling zero count.** Since T10a restricts sibling production to `inc(·, 0)` and every output of a conforming allocator satisfies T4 (Consequence 4), TA5-SigValid fixes `sig(tₙ) = #tₙ` at every step; T4's field-segment constraint forces the terminal component non-zero, hence strictly positive (T0's carrier ℕ). The `inc(·, 0)` step then advances only that position (TA5(b), TA5(c)), leaving its non-zero status intact, and preserves length (TA5(c)), so no position enters or leaves the zero set. Every sibling output therefore shares the base address's zero count (T10a.8). This is the zero-count parallel of Consequence 1's length uniformity; together they supply the full *base address → every sibling* extension of per-base-address TA5(d) bookkeeping on both length (Consequence 1) and zero count (Consequence 8) that cross-allocator reasoning consumes at the domain level rather than only at base addresses.

**Necessity.** Suppose the discipline is relaxed to permit any k ≥ 0 in the sibling stream. Let an allocator with base address b produce a₁ = inc(b, 0), then a₂ = inc(a₁, k') with k' > 0 — both legitimate sibling outputs under the relaxed rule. By TA5(b), since k' > 0, a₂ agrees with a₁ on every position 1 ≤ i ≤ #a₁. By TA5(d), #a₂ = #a₁ + k' > #a₁, so a₂ has components beyond position #a₁. These two facts — agreement on all positions of the shorter tumbler and strict length excess in the longer — are precisely the conditions of the Prefix definition: `a₁ ≼ a₂` with `#a₁ < #a₂`, so `a₁ ≺ a₂`. The relaxed rule therefore admits siblings a₁ ≺ a₂, falsifying T10a.2 (NonNestingSiblingPrefixes) — the within-allocator claim that distinct siblings of one allocator are prefix-incomparable (T10a-N). T10a.2 is an internal integrity property of T10a's sibling stream and is distinct from T10's non-nesting precondition `p₁ ⋠ p₂ ∧ p₂ ⋠ p₁`, which is applied at the domain prefixes of two distinct allocators (as in Case 3 of GlobalUniqueness) and is supplied for non-ancestor-descendant pairs by T10a.5 (CrossAllocatorIncomparability); co-siblings of one allocator are not such a pair, so the failure of T10a.2 is a failure of T10a's own within-allocator guarantee rather than a failure at T10's inputs. The sibling restriction — `k = 0` only — is thus necessary for within-allocator prefix-incomparability (T10a.2). The remaining components of the axiom serve distinct purposes not addressed by this argument: the child-spawning bound `k' ∈ {1, 2}` is necessary for T4 preservation (Consequence 4, via TA5a's failure for `k ≥ 3`), the at-most-once child-spawning constraint ensures uniqueness of child base addresses (Consequence 5), and the root T4 initialization seeds the inductive argument of Consequence 4. The discipline is sufficient for prefix-incomparability across all non-ancestor-descendant allocator output domains (T10a.1, T10a.3–T10a.5); the necessity proof establishes that its sibling restriction is indispensable to T10a.2, the within-allocator half of that guarantee. ∎

*Formal Contract:*
- *Definitions:*
  - *Allocator tree* `𝒯`: the set of allocators induced by T10a. Consists of a distinguished root together with every allocator reachable by finite iteration of the child-spawning rule. Each non-root allocator `A` carries a *spawning triple* `(parent(A), spawnPt(A), spawnParam(A))` with `parent(A) ∈ 𝒯`, `spawnPt(A) ∈ dom(parent(A))`, and `spawnParam(A) ∈ {1, 2}`; A's base address is `inc(spawnPt(A), spawnParam(A))` by TA5. The root has no spawning triple; its base address is the T4-valid address fixed by the initialization constraint.
  - *Identity of allocators:* `X = Y` iff both are the root, or both are non-root with identical spawning triples — `parent(X) = parent(Y)`, `spawnPt(X) = spawnPt(Y)`, `spawnParam(X) = spawnParam(Y)`. Equivalently, `X = Y` iff their root-path sequences of spawning triples coincide. Distinctness is `X ≠ Y`. The at-most-once child-spawning constraint ensures each `(parent, t, k')` names at most one allocator.
  - *Derived tree relations:* `Y` is a *child* of `X` iff `parent(Y) = X`. The depth function satisfies `depth(root) = 0` and `depth(Y) = depth(parent(Y)) + 1`. `X` is an *ancestor* of `Y` iff `X = Y` or `X` is an ancestor of `parent(Y)`; `Y` is a *descendant* of `X` iff `X` is an ancestor of `Y`. `X` and `Y` are *in an ancestor-descendant relationship* iff one is an ancestor of the other. A *param-1 child* of `X` is a child with `spawnParam = 1`; a *param-2 child* is one with `spawnParam = 2`. The *param-k* prefix names the spawn parameter and is distinct from the tree-level `depth` function; a param-1 child `C` and a param-2 child `C'` of the same parent both satisfy `depth(C) = depth(C') = depth(X) + 1`.
  - *Domain:* `dom(A) = {tₙ : n ≥ 0}` where `t₀` is `A`'s base address and `tₙ₊₁ = inc(tₙ, 0)` — the `inc(·, 0)` chain extending the base. Child-spawning outputs `inc(t, k')` with `k' > 0` are excluded from the parent's domain and serve as the base of the child allocator `C` (with `parent(C) = parent-allocator`, `spawnPt(C) = t`, `spawnParam(C) = k'`), becoming the initial element of `dom(C)`.
  - *Same allocator:* `same_allocator(a, b) ≡ ∃A ∈ 𝒯 : a ∈ dom(A) ∧ b ∈ dom(A)` — the predicate asserting that `a` and `b` both lie in some allocator's domain, with the existential ranging over the well-defined tree 𝒯.
- *Axiom:* The root allocator's base address satisfies T4. Allocators produce sibling outputs exclusively by `inc(·, 0)`; child-spawning uses exactly one `inc(·, k')` with `k' ∈ {1, 2}`, subject to the runtime precondition — imposed by the discipline and checked before each spawn — that `zeros(t) ≤ 3` when `k' = 1` and `zeros(t) ≤ 2` when `k' = 2`, where `zeros(·)` is the zero-count function defined in T4. The chosen `inc(·, k')` establishes the child's prefix, after which the parent resumes sibling production with `inc(·, 0)`. Each `(t, k')` pair — domain element and spawning parameter — yields at most one child-spawning event. Depends: T4 (HierarchicalParsing — the definition site of `zeros(·)` that the axiom names in its runtime precondition, and the invariant the root must satisfy and the discipline must preserve), TA5 (HierarchicalIncrement — defines `inc` and its length/positional postconditions), TA5a (IncrementPreservesT4 — supplies the preservation bounds `k' = 1 ∧ zeros(t) ≤ 3` and `k' = 2 ∧ zeros(t) ≤ 2` that the discipline adopts as runtime constraints on child-spawning parameters, so every conforming spawn lands on a T4-preserving input).
- *Postconditions:*
  - T10a.1 (Uniform sibling length): For every allocator with base address b, all sibling outputs a satisfy #a = #b. Depends: TA5 (TA5(c): #inc(t, 0) = #t).
  - T10a.2 (Non-nesting sibling prefixes): For all siblings a, b from the same allocator, same_allocator(a, b) ∧ a ≠ b → a and b are prefix-incomparable. Depends: T10a.1 (#a = #b) and Prefix (equal-length tumblers are prefix-related only if identical).
  - T10a.3 (Length separation): For every child allocator spawned by `inc(·, k')` with k' ∈ {1, 2} from a parent with base length m, all child outputs c satisfy #c = m + k', and across d nesting levels the separation is exact: #output = m + k'₁ + k'₂ + … + k'_d. Local monotonicity: for any ancestor-descendant pair (A, B) on a lineage, #output(B) > #output(A). Depends: T10a.1 (uniform sibling length) and TA5 (TA5(d): #inc(t, k') = #t + k' for k' > 0).
  - T10a.4 (T4 preservation): The root allocator's base address satisfies T4 (initialization constraint); since `inc(·, 0)` unconditionally preserves T4 (TA5a) and child-spawning uses `k' ∈ {1, 2}` within TA5a bounds, every output of a conforming allocator satisfies T4 by induction on the allocator tree. Depends: T4 (HierarchicalParsing — the invariant preserved by induction), TA5a (inc(·, 0) preserves T4 unconditionally; inc(·, k') with k' ∈ {1, 2} preserves T4 within zero-count bounds).
  - T10a.5 (Cross-allocator prefix-incomparability): For any two allocators X and Y not in an ancestor-descendant relationship, for all x ∈ domain(X) and y ∈ domain(Y), x ⋠ y ∧ y ⋠ x. Depends: T10a (at-most-once constraint), T10a.1 (uniform length), T10a.3 (length separation), T10a.4 (T4 preservation), T4 (HierarchicalParsing — TA5-SigValid precondition), TA5 (increment postconditions), TA5-SigValid (sig = length for T4-valid addresses), T3 (divergence of distinct tumblers), Prefix (prefix definition). This alone delivers T10's non-nesting precondition `p₁ ⋠ p₂ ∧ p₂ ⋠ p₁` at the domain prefixes of every non-ancestor-descendant allocator pair in the tree, since T10's precondition is a cross-allocator condition and T10a.2 — the within-allocator incomparability of co-siblings — does not participate in supplying it. Ancestor-descendant pairs inherently nest (TA5(b), TA5(d)); their output distinctness follows from length separation (T10a.3).
  - T10a.6 (Domain disjointness): For any two distinct allocators X and Y, dom(X) ∩ dom(Y) = ∅. Case 1 (ancestor–descendant): T10a.1 + T10a.3 — uniform per-domain length (T10a.1) combined with the strict length inequality between ancestor and descendant streams (T10a.3) excludes any common element. Case 2 (non-ancestor–descendant): T10a.5 + Prefix reflexivity — cross-allocator prefix-incomparability (T10a.5) is contradicted by `t ≼ t` were `t` shared. Witness-uniqueness corollary: when `same_allocator(a, b)` holds, the witnessing allocator A is uniquely determined by the pair (a, b). Single-valuedness of the enumeration indices i, j with a = tᵢ, b = tⱼ requires also the within-allocator injectivity of T10a.7; the two results together underwrite well-definedness of T9's allocated_before. Depends: T10a.1 (uniform sibling length), T10a.3 (length separation), T10a.5 (cross-allocator prefix-incomparability), Prefix (reflexivity of ≼).
  - T10a.7 (Enumeration injectivity): For every allocator A with domain `dom(A) = {tₙ : n ≥ 0}` where `tₙ₊₁ = inc(tₙ, 0)`, the indexing map `n ↦ tₙ` is injective: `m ≠ n → tₘ ≠ tₙ`. Proof: TA5(a) gives per-step strict increase `tₙ₊₁ > tₙ`; induction on the gap via T1 (c) transitivity yields `tₘ < tₙ` for `m < n`; T1 (a) irreflexivity converts strict inequality into distinctness. Equivalently, the enumeration is strictly increasing under the tumbler order. Together with T10a.6, this makes the enumeration indices `i, j` with `a = tᵢ, b = tⱼ` single-valued functions of the pair `(a, b)` whenever `same_allocator(a, b)` holds, supplying the second of the two pieces required for well-definedness of T9's `allocated_before`. Depends: T10a (enumeration definition), TA5 (TA5(a): `inc(t, 0) > t`), T1 (T1 (a): irreflexivity; T1 (c): transitivity).
  - T10a.8 (Uniform sibling zero count): For every allocator with base address `b`, all sibling outputs `a` satisfy `zeros(a) = zeros(b)`. Proof: T10a.4 gives T4-validity at every sibling, TA5-SigValid fixes `sig(tₙ) = #tₙ`, and T4's field-segment constraint (with T0's ℕ carrier) forces that position strictly positive; TA5(b)/TA5(c) modify only that position and preserve length, so the `+1` step keeps it positive and the zero set is unchanged. The zero-count parallel of T10a.1 — extending a base-address zero count (fixed by TA5(d) at child-spawning) to every sibling in the allocator's domain — as the length parallel extends base-address length to every sibling. Depends: T10a (sibling production restricted to `inc(·, 0)`), T10a.4 (T4-validity at every output, supplying the preconditions of TA5-SigValid and T4's field-segment constraint at each step), T4 (HierarchicalParsing — `t_{#t} ≠ 0` forces the sig-position strictly positive), TA5 (TA5(b) preserves non-sig positions; TA5(c) advances sig by `+1` and preserves length), TA5-SigValid (`sig(t) = #t` for T4-valid `t`, identifying the advanced position as the terminal component).
  - T10a-N (Necessity of sibling restriction): Under the relaxed rule (any k ≥ 0 in the sibling stream), the pair a₁ = inc(b, 0) and a₂ = inc(a₁, k') with k' > 0 are sibling outputs satisfying a₁ ≺ a₂ — by TA5(b) (agreement on all positions of a₁) and TA5(d) (#a₂ > #a₁), invoking the Prefix definition. This falsifies T10a.2 (NonNestingSiblingPrefixes) — T10a's within-allocator consequence that distinct sibling outputs of one allocator are prefix-incomparable. T10a.2 is a within-allocator claim and does not supply T10's precondition `p₁ ⋠ p₂ ∧ p₂ ⋠ p₁`, which is applied at the domain prefixes of two distinct allocators (GlobalUniqueness Case 3) and is supplied for non-ancestor-descendant pairs by T10a.5 (CrossAllocatorIncomparability); co-siblings of one allocator are not such a pair, so the failure is internal to T10a and does not propagate through T10. The sibling restriction (`k = 0` only) is therefore necessary for the within-allocator prefix-incomparability of T10a.2. The remaining axiom components — the `k' ∈ {1, 2}` child-spawning bound, the at-most-once constraint, and the root T4 initialization — serve T4 preservation (TA5a) and child-prefix uniqueness respectively; their necessity for those distinct purposes is not addressed by this argument. Depends: TA5 (TA5(b): agreement on positions 1..#t; TA5(d): #inc(t, k') = #t + k'), Prefix (prefix definition), T10a.2 (NonNestingSiblingPrefixes — the within-allocator consequence directly falsified), T10a.5 (CrossAllocatorIncomparability — cited for orientation as the property that actually supplies T10's cross-allocator precondition, disjoint from T10a.2), and T10 (PartitionIndependence — cited only for orientation; its precondition is cross-allocator and is therefore not what this necessity argument falsifies).
