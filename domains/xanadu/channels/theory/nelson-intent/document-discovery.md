# Document Discovery

Source: Literary Machines, 2/46-2/50, 4/23-4/24, 4/41, 4/60

## What It Means

Nelson envisions document discovery as a fundamentally different model from both traditional library catalogs and web search engines. There is **no central catalog**, **no system directory**, and **no full-text search** built into the core system. Instead, discovery emerges from the structure of the docuverse itself through three complementary mechanisms:

1. **User-published directories** - Anyone can create and publish document lists, categorized however they wish
2. **Bidirectional link traversal** - From any document, find everything that links TO it
3. **Address-space navigation** - Browse by author (account) or server (node)

The key insight: in Xanadu, the **links ARE the catalog**. Rather than a separate indexing system maintained by authorities, the web of connections created by users themselves becomes the primary means of finding content.

## User Guarantee

- **No gatekeepers** - Anyone can publish directories; no one controls the "official" catalog
- **Bidirectional discovery** - For any document, you can ask "What links here?"
- **Author/node browsing** - You can find all documents by an author or on a server
- **Filter by attributes** - Sieve in-links by title, location, author, time
- **Social navigation** - Follow links through the community's curated pathways

## Principle Served

**Literature is self-organizing.** Rather than imposing top-down classification (Dewey Decimal, Library of Congress), Xanadu allows bottom-up organization through the collective link-making activity of all users. This reflects Nelson's view that categorization systems become obsolete ("have a half-life") while actual connections between documents are permanently meaningful.

## The Directory Document Model

Nelson explicitly rejects system-level directories:

> "For instance, there is no system directory; rather, we encourage the on-line publishing of directory documents by users." (4/41)

This means:
- Anyone can publish a categorized list of documents
- Multiple competing directories can exist for the same topic
- Directories are just documents - they can be linked to, transcluded, annotated
- Users vote with their attention on which directories are useful
- No one has authority to say "this is THE directory"

## Link-Based Discovery

The bidirectional link system enables powerful discovery:

> "The reader should be able to ask, for a given document or place in the document, 'What connects here from other documents?'-- and be shown all these outside connections without appreciable delay." (2/46)

This is filtered by attributes:

> "This filtering by different attributes we call 'sieving', and it can only be set up for a comparatively small number of traits-- say, location and author and time." (2/47)

The search model from 4/60:

> "THE QUANTITY OF LINKS NOT SATISFYING A REQUEST DOES NOT IN PRINCIPLE IMPEDE SEARCH ON OTHERS."

This means link-based discovery scales: even with millions of links, finding the ones that matter remains fast.

## Address-Space Navigation

Because tumbler addresses encode hierarchy, you can browse structurally:

> "A link to or search of an account or node will find any of the documents under it." (4/23)

This enables:
- "Show me all documents by this author" (account range)
- "Show me all documents on this server" (node range)
- "Show me all versions of this document" (version range)

## Why No Text Search?

Nelson does not specify full-text keyword search as a core system feature. This is deliberate:

1. **Links are semantically richer** - A link says "this relates to that" in a typed way; keyword matching is just string coincidence
2. **User-created organization** - Humans curate better catalogs than algorithms
3. **Separation of concerns** - The back-end handles storage and links; search could be a front-end or middle-end service

That said, Nelson does not FORBID text search - it could be a service built on top of the base system. It's just not specified as a core guarantee.

## The Subculture Factor

Nelson also envisions a **social layer** to discovery through the "subculture of intellect" - the Xanadu Hypercorps staffing Xanadu Stands:

> "Like good woodsmen they will have a sense of the trails and byways of the territory to be explored."

The human experts help users navigate the docuverse, recommend paths, and curate access points. Discovery is not purely technical - it's mediated by community.

## Nelson's Words

On directory documents:
> "For instance, there is no system directory; rather, we encourage the on-line publishing of directory documents by users." (4/41)

On browsing address ranges:
> "A link to or search of an account or node will find any of the documents under it." (4/23)

On user-created directories:
> "Anyone may publish his or her own document lists, categorized in any way he or she imagines, and have users browse through them in search of whatever they think they find." (2/50)

On bidirectional link discovery:
> "The reader should be able to ask, for a given document or place in the document, 'What connects here from other documents?'-- and be shown all these outside connections without appreciable delay." (2/46)

On filtering:
> "Thus it becomes necessary to apply some kind of filter, saying, 'What links come in from Spain? From last week? From persons of importance to me?'" (2/47)

On category systems decaying:
> "There is nothing wrong with categorization. It is, however, by its nature transient: category systems have a half-life, and categorizations begin to look fairly stupid after a few years." (2/49)

On link search efficiency:
> "THE QUANTITY OF LINKS NOT SATISFYING A REQUEST DOES NOT IN PRINCIPLE IMPEDE SEARCH ON OTHERS." (4/60)
