# Indexing

Source: Literary Machines, 3/23-3/24 (pages 165-166)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Nelson envisions **Xanadu indexing** as a universal annotation and cross-reference system that applies to all media—not just text. Movies, music, photographs, and interactive presentations all need the same capability that literary scholarship has developed for text: multiple commentators adding to a common pool of knowledge, interconnections tracked automatically, and the ability to navigate from any work to everything that references it.

The key insight: current media systems are **closed**. Interactive videodiscs exist but "only as closed systems to which others may not add or make variations." Film archives have no way for different scholars to annotate the same footage in a shared system. Music catalogues don't connect to commentary, quotation, or performance. This fragmentation is "unacceptable in the long term."

### User Guarantee

- **Open annotation** - Multiple people can annotate the same media without interfering with each other
- **Interconnection** - From any piece, find everything that references, quotes, or comments on it
- **Media independence** - The same indexing applies to text, film, music, images, and interactive works

### Principle Served

**All knowledge is interconnected.** Just as scientific literature has mechanisms for citation and bibliography, all media need systems for tracking how works relate to each other. The hypertext vision isn't limited to text—it's about making the web of human knowledge explicit and navigable across all forms.

### How Users Experience It

- Watch a film → access annotations from multiple scholars without leaving the viewing experience
- Listen to a Mozart piece → find every document mentioning it, every composition quoting it, every recording
- View archival footage → see how it's been used in different productions
- Create commentary → your annotations become part of the shared knowledge base, linked to what they comment on

### Nelson's Words

> "There as yet exists no sensible means by which motion pictures may be annotated by different commentators in a common bank of writings. Similarly, there exist no good film-editing systems that support the intercomparison of complex alternatives." (3/23)

> "Tomorrow's nonsequential movie media--hypermovies and 'interactive videodiscs'--require new depths of access by users. Though interactive videodiscs have begun to proliferate, it is only as closed systems to which others may not add or make variations. This is unacceptable in the long term." (3/23)

> "Xanadu indexing is needed for films and video, from raw footage through editing to archive, and for on-line interactive presentations of every kind." (3/23)

> "From a given Mozart piece, say, it will be possible to get to every document which mentions or comments on that piece; every musical composition which quotes from that piece; and every recording of that piece or its derivatives." (3/24)

### The Vision Beyond Text

Nelson's examples reveal the ambition: film annotation across multiple scholars, music cataloguing connected to performance and commentary, archival footage linked to all its uses. This isn't about file organization—it's about making the web of references that exists informally in human culture into something explicit and navigable by machine.

The contrast with "closed systems" is deliberate. Proprietary interactive videodiscs, academic databases, music catalogues—each creates a silo. Xanadu indexing means one system where everything connects to everything else, regardless of media type.
