# Privacy

Source: Literary Machines, 2/59-2/60

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Privacy in Xanadu is a firm commitment: the network itself must not spy on its users. Nelson declares this not as a feature but as a constraint on the system's design - it "may not monitor what is read or what is written in private documents." This extends the printing press analogy: just as no one tracks what books you read in your library, Xanadu must preserve that same sanctuary for electronic documents.

Nelson acknowledges this is difficult to guarantee technically. But the principle is non-negotiable for a system he describes as fundamentally libertarian.

### User Guarantee

- The network will not monitor what you read
- The network will not monitor what you write in private documents
- You can publish anonymously ("John Doe publication")
- Your private documents remain truly private

### Principle Served

**Freedom of information as a civil right.** Nelson frames Xanadu as "the printing press of the future" and insists it must inherit printing's freedoms. If Xanadu is a printing press, it can brook "no greater restriction on its functions than on conventional printing." The political battle for freedom of electronic information, he notes, has already begun - and the system must be designed to resist surveillance and censorship.

This is a libertarian system: "restrict it, and all will lose."

### How Users Experience It

- Read any published document without the system recording your reading history
- Write private documents knowing the system itself cannot peek at contents
- Publish anonymously when you choose ("John Doe publication")
- Maintain accountable identity when you want reputation and legal protection
- Challenge anonymous publications that defame you (peremptory challenge)

### Anonymous Publication

Nelson explicitly supports anonymous publication:

> "Normally publication will be by accountable individuals and companies with known residence or place of business. Still, there is no reason that anonymous publication by walk-in and transient users of this system should not be allowed. We may call this 'John Doe publication.'"

However, anonymous publication comes with vulnerability to challenge. Since the John Doe author won't "hang around to defend their acts," affected individuals can seek removal through peremptory challenge. This balances free speech against accountability.

For accountable publishers (identified individuals or firms), peremptory challenge "no longer holds water" - removal requires negotiation or court order.

### The Libertarian Foundation

Nelson is explicit about the system's political character:

> "There is no thinking out all these eventualities. But the system we propose is a libertarian system: restrict it, and all will lose."

The system must live within the law, but Nelson worries about grey areas: pornography, libel, "national security" (which he notes often means "matters embarrassing to a political administration"). He sees the political battle for electronic freedom as already underway.

### Nelson's Words

On the core principle:

> "The network will not, may not monitor what is read or what is written in private documents. But these matters are not easy to guarantee."

On the printing press analogy:

> "We consider that this system may best be considered as the printing press of the future."

On freedom:

> "If this system is a printing press, we can brook no greater restriction on its functions than on conventional printing. Freedom of the press has been challenged by tyrants and scoundrels since the time of Gutenberg. Such challenges will happen again, and worse, on this new playing-field. We must be ready."

On anonymous publication:

> "Normally publication will be by accountable individuals and companies with known residence or place of business. Still, there is no reason that anonymous publication by walk-in and transient users of this system should not be allowed. We may call this 'John Doe publication.'"

On challenging anonymous content:

> "Consider libelous or uncomplimentary John Doe graffiti defaming specific individuals. If John Doe is not available, the affected individual must be able to effect removal of the materials by peremptory challenge."

On the libertarian character:

> "There is no thinking out all these eventualities. But the system we propose is a libertarian system: restrict it, and all will lose."
