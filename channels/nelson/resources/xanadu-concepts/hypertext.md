# Hypertext

Source: Literary Machines, 3/2-3/7 (pages 144-149)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Hypertext is non-sequential writing—text that branches and allows reader choice. Nelson's Xanadu Hypertext System extends this into a comprehensive form of storage that clarifies computer use and enables new forms of electronic publication.

The system is not merely a way of organizing text; it is a unified approach to storing and delivering all forms of content—text, graphics, pictures, musical notations, photographs, recordings—with previously impossible arrangements and interconnections. The key insight is that literature has never been truly sequential, and computers finally give us the ability to represent its true interconnected nature.

### User Guarantee

- **Any form of content** can be stored: text, pictures, musical notations, photographs, recordings, and other digital information
- **Links and windows** connect documents, enabling non-sequential navigation
- **Commonalities revealed**: the system clarifies what different documents and versions share
- **Any available document** accessible from any computer in the network at any time
- **Users connect their own machines** to the system; each machine uses its own front-end software
- **Contents are controlled** by their owners—private documents remain private, published documents are available to anyone

### Principle Served

People mistake generality for vagueness. The Xanadu Hypertext System is general because literature itself is general—it encompasses all forms of interconnected human expression. The purpose is to represent the true shape of information: "A vessel for the true shape of information—without having to cut it or jam it."

This generality serves multiple needs simultaneously:
- A mapping system between storage and virtual documents
- A distributed server network for pooled documents
- A storage arrangement for linking arbitrary collections of material
- A seamless data architecture for linked electronic publishing
- A grand address space for everything, parts of which can be in different places at once

### How Users Experience It

- **Non-sequential reading**: Follow links to move through material in any order, based on interest
- **Historical backtrack**: Return to where you came from
- **Windows between documents**: See quoted material in context, with connection to original
- **Alternative versions**: Access and compare different versions of the same material
- **Promiscuous linkage**: System permits linking and windowing among all materials
- **Panning and zooming**: Bit-map graphics support continuous scrolling and magnification
- **Collage composition**: Three-dimensional objects can be assembled from compound objects, scenes from history, enactments, and artwork
- **Published vs Private**: Private documents available only to owner and designees; published documents available to anyone with royalty going to owner
- **Maintained connective structures**: Unlike other systems, links, windows, alternative versions, and historical backtrack survive as documents change

## Differentiation from Other Systems

Nelson explicitly contrasts Xanadu with systems like "The Source" (a general-purpose time-sharing service from that era):

- The Source runs many kinds of programs and furnishes text services as "one class of available program"
- Xanadu is "a specialized storage form for what we believe is the most generalized form of storage"
- The Source does not permit running user programs
- The Source does not support: linkage, windows, alternative versions, historical backtrack
- The Source does not "maintain those connective structures as documents change"

The critical distinction: other systems treat text as static files. Xanadu maintains the *relationships* between content even as content changes.
