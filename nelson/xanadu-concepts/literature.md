# Literature

Source: Literary Machines, 2/7-2/11 (pages 087-091)

## Semantic Intent

### What It Means

Nelson redefines **literature** not as "belles lettres or leather-bound books" but as **any system of interconnected writings**. Scientific papers, business correspondence, legal documents—all are literature because they reference, quote, respond to, and build upon each other.

This is not a metaphor but "a discovered fact": writing doesn't exist in isolation. Every document connects to others through citation, quotation, response, and shared context. The structure of these interconnections IS literature.

The implication for Xanadu: if literature is inherently interconnected, then the system that stores it must preserve and expose those connections—not hide them as traditional file systems do.

### User Guarantee

- **Connections are visible** - Links between documents are part of the system, not hidden in footnotes
- **Context is preserved** - When you read something, you can find what it responds to and what responds to it
- **Literature evolves** - Documents can be reinterpreted, commented on, and built upon without destroying the original

### Principle Served

**Writing is not isolated.** Traditional storage treats documents as independent files. But Nelson observes that "almost all writing is part of some literature"—a web of interconnections. Xanadu makes this web explicit and navigable.

### How Users Experience It

- Read an article → see what it cites and what cites it
- Write a response → your document links back to what you're responding to
- Follow a debate → trace the chain of documents in conversation
- Find related work → discover connections you didn't know existed

### Nelson's Words

> "By the term 'literature' we are not necessarily talking about belles lettres or leather-bound books. We mean it in the same broad sense of 'the scientific literature,' or that graduate-school question, 'Have you looked at the literature?'" (2/9)

> "A literature is a system of interconnected writings. We do not offer this as our definition, but as a discovered fact. And almost all writing is part of some literature." (2/9)

> "In any ongoing literature, there is perpetual reinterpretation and reinterpretation, and links between documents help us follow the connections." (2/10)

> "That structure is literature." (2/7)

> "the structure of that world--the system of interconnections of its stored materials-- is the same from screen to screen, no matter how a given screen may show it." (2/7)

### LITERATURE IS DEBUGGED

Nelson makes a striking claim: literature is **debugged** through its interconnections. Scientific publication, with its mechanisms of quotation, citation, and bibliography, has been "carefully adjusted in an evolving process." Errors get corrected through responses; ideas get refined through debate.

> "In the evolution of the printed word, the mechanisms of quotation, citation and bibliography have been carefully adjusted in an evolving process. Scientific publication has one set of conventions; supermarket tabloids are much vaguer about sources, and most publications are somewhere in between." (2/10)

This means Xanadu's linking isn't just convenient—it continues the centuries-long process of making literature self-correcting.
