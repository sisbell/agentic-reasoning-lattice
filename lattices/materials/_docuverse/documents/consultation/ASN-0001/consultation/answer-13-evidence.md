## Question 13 [evidence]

> Do any substances in the data show a roughly constant product of specific heat and atomic weight across different elements?

Yes. The corpus tabulates this product explicitly for 13 elements [1819-dulong-petit].

| Substance | Specific heat | Relative atomic weight | Product |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|---------|
| Bismuth | 0.0288 | 13.30 | 0.3830 |
| Lead | 0.0293 | 12.95 | 0.3794 |
| Gold | 0.0298 | 12.43 | 0.3704 |
| Platinum | 0.0314 | 11.16 | 0.3740 |
| Tin | 0.0514 | 7.35 | 0.3779 |
| Silver | 0.0557 | 6.75 | 0.3759 |
| Zinc | 0.0927 | 4.03 | 0.3736 |
| Tellurium | 0.0912 | 4.03 | 0.3675 |
| Copper | 0.0949 | 3.957 | 0.3755 |
| Nickel | 0.1035 | 3.69 | 0.3819 |
| Iron | 0.1100 | 3.392 | 0.3731 |
| Cobalt | 0.1498 | 2.46 | 0.3685 |
| Sulphur | 0.1880 | 2.011 | 0.3780 |

The products range from **0.3675** (Tellurium) to **0.3830** (Bismuth), a spread of roughly 4% across substances whose specific heats vary by nearly a factor of 7 and atomic weights by a factor of 6.6. No units are stated for either specific heat or atomic weight in the source; the product is dimensionless relative to the authors' reference standard.

The corpus explicitly labels these products "the capacities of the different atoms" and states that their near-equality is the basis for the law announced: *"The atoms of all simple bodies have exactly the same capacity for heat."* [1819-dulong-petit]
