## Question 9 [theory]

> What does the theory predict about the sharing of vis viva between two bodies of different constitution brought into thermal contact?

# What the theory predicts about sharing of *vis viva* between bodies of different constitution in thermal contact

## The central result: equal mean *vis viva* per molecule

The corpus states that when two systems of molecules interact freely, the mean translational *vis viva* per molecule becomes equal across the two systems. Maxwell derives this twice — once by a direct argument about pressure and temperature, and once from the final distribution of velocities.

From the direct argument: "the final result in the case of hard elastic bodies is to cause the average *vis viva* of a molecule to be the same for all the different kinds of molecules... When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

From the final distribution (equations 33–34): "When there are two systems of molecules $M_1\alpha^2 = M_2\beta^2$, whence $M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$, or the mean *vis viva* of a molecule will be the same in each system" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Independence from the law of force

Maxwell emphasizes this is a constitution-independent result: "This is a very important result in the theory of gases, and it is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules, as are all the other results relating to the final distribution of velocities" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. It holds "whatever theory we adopt as to the nature of the action between the individual molecules."

## Approach to equilibrium

For two gases in contact, the difference in mean *vis viva* per molecule decays exponentially. With $Q_1 = \frac{M_1}{M_1+M_2}(\overline{\xi_1^2}+\overline{\eta_1^2}+\overline{\zeta_1^2})$ and analogously $Q_2$, Maxwell derives $Q_2 - Q_1 = Ce^{-nt}$, so "if the gases are in contact and undisturbed, $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ will rapidly become equal. Now the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Consequence: Law of Equivalent Volumes

Equality of mean molecular *vis viva*, combined with the kinetic expression for pressure, yields Gay-Lussac's law: "if the pressures as well as the temperatures be the same in two gases, $\frac{M_1}{\rho_1} = \frac{M_2}{\rho_2}$, or the masses of the individual molecules are proportional to the density of the gas" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Role of internal degrees of freedom

The *vis viva* that equalizes is the translational part (motion of the centre of gravity). Internal motion (rotation, vibration) is treated via Clausius's assumption that "the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value $(\beta - 1)$" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], so total molecular energy is $\frac{1}{2}\beta M v^2$ with $\beta$ depending on the molecule's constitution. Different constitutions can therefore carry different *total* energies per molecule at the same temperature, even though translational *vis viva* per molecule is equal.

## Extension beyond gases — explicitly tentative

Maxwell extends the principle cautiously: "A law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. So for bodies that are not both gases, the corpus asserts only that *some* similar energy-sharing law likely governs equilibrium; the precise form is not given.

## Summary

For two gases of different constitution brought into thermal contact, the theory predicts that exchange of motion through molecular encounters drives the mean translational *vis viva* per molecule to a common value — this equality *defines* equality of temperature, is independent of the intermolecular force law, and entails the Law of Equivalent Volumes. For liquids or solids, the corpus conjectures an analogous law but does not specify it.
