## Question 14 [evidence]

> What relative atomic weights do the authors assign to each of the thirteen simple bodies, and on what empirical basis (chemical combining proportions) are these weights fixed?

## Relative Atomic Weights in Dulong & Petit (1819)

From the table in `[1819-dulong-petit]`, the thirteen simple bodies and their assigned relative atomic weights are:

| Substance | Relative atomic weight |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Bismuth | 13.30 |
| Lead | 12.95 |
| Gold | 12.43 |
| Platinum | 11.16 |
| Tin | 7.35 |
| Silver | 6.75 |
| Zinc | 4.03 |
| Tellurium | 4.03 |
| Copper | 3.957 |
| Nickel | 3.69 |
| Iron | 3.392 |
| Cobalt | 2.46 |
| Sulphur | 2.011 |

These are dimensionless relative weights, not absolute masses.

**Empirical basis:** The authors state the weights "are deduced, as is known, from the ratios observed between the weights of the elementary substances that unite together" — that is, from measured chemical combining proportions (stoichiometric mass ratios from compound formation) `[1819-dulong-petit]`.

**Known ambiguity:** The authors explicitly acknowledge that because no method then existed to determine the actual number of atoms entering a combination, "there must always be something arbitrary in the choice of the specific weight of the elementary molecules" — the uncertainty being only whether to choose a value or a simple integer multiple of it. They state their choices were guided by "the best established chemical analogies" and, implicitly, by selecting whichever assignment made the law (equal atomic heat capacity) hold most cleanly `[1819-dulong-petit]`.
