## What Distinguishes Simple from Compound Bodies

The authors draw the line operationally, not philosophically. For simple bodies, atomic weights can be derived from measured combination ratios: "the relative weights of their atoms are deduced from the ratios observed between the weights of the elementary substances that unite together." There remains "something arbitrary in the choice of the specific weight of the elementary molecules," but only within "two or three numbers which have the most simple relation to each other" — a bounded ambiguity they resolve by appeal to chemical analogy.

For compound bodies, the situation is worse: "no precise method exists of discovering the real number of atoms of each kind which enter into a combination," making compound atomic weights "purely arbitrary." Because the product (specific heat × atomic weight) is the key quantity in the law, an undetermined atomic weight makes the product uninterpretable. The authors therefore set compounds aside explicitly: "we choose rather to wait till the new order of considerations which we have just established can be applied to a sufficiently great number of bodies... that the opinion adopted may be founded on decisive conclusions."

Compound bodies mentioned but not tabulated include water, sulphuret of lead, oxides of copper/lead/silver, carburet of sulphur, nitric acid, and nitre. All appear only in qualitative discussion of heat released during combination, not in the specific-heat table [1819-dulong-petit].
