## Question 6 [theory]

> How does the theory distinguish between the vis viva of molecular translation and the vis viva associated with motion internal to each molecule at equilibrium?

The corpus distinguishes the two components through a decomposition of molecular energy and a single ratio β.

**Two components of molecular energy.** "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity. If the body is of invariable form, the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity consist entirely of rotation, but if the parts of the body are not rigidly connected, their motions may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**The ratio β.** "The whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation. The ratio β will be different for every molecule, and will be different for the same molecule after every encounter with another molecule, but it will have an average value depending on the nature of the molecules, as has been shown by Clausius" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. For pure centres of force, "there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation."

**Equilibrium assumption.** The translational and internal shares settle to a fixed proportion through encounters: "in a great many encounters the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Equivalently: "If we assume with Clausius that the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1) … this ratio is always preserved, so that Ē = (β − 1)(ξ² + η² + ζ²)" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The total invisible-agitation energy per unit volume is then ½β(ξ² + η² + ζ²)ρ = (3/2)βp.

**Only translation equalizes between species.** Temperature equality between two gases is defined by the flow of vis viva of translation being zero. The derivation in the "Law of Equivalent Volumes" section yields M₁(ξ₁² + η₁² + ζ₁²) = M₂(ξ₂² + η₂² + ζ₂²), i.e. equal *translational* mean vis viva per molecule, from which "equal volumes of different gases at equal pressures and temperatures contain equal numbers of molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The internal share is carried along by each species at its own β, and "the value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them" — formalized later as β = (2/3)·1/(γ − 1) [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

So the theory keeps the two vis vivas separate in bookkeeping (via β), treats only the translational part as what equalizes across species at thermal equilibrium, and leaves the internal part fixed by a molecule-specific ratio inferred from specific heats rather than computed from the dynamics — the corpus explicitly notes "we have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].
