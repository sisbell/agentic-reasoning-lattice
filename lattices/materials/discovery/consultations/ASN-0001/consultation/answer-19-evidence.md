## Question 19 [evidence]

> Do any substances in the data deviate from otherwise regular heat-capacity patterns, and what are the outliers?

## Deviations from the equal-atomic-heat-capacity pattern

The paper's own test for regularity is the product (atomic weight × specific heat), which the authors claim should be constant across all elements. The 13-substance table yields these products [1819-dulong-petit]:

| Substance | Product |
|-----------|---------|
| Bismuth | 0.3830 |
| Nickel | 0.3819 |
| Lead | 0.3794 |
| Sulphur | 0.3780 |
| Tin | 0.3779 |
| Silver | 0.3759 |
| Copper | 0.3755 |
| Zinc | 0.3736 |
| Iron | 0.3731 |
| Platinum | 0.3740 |
| Gold | 0.3704 |
| Cobalt | 0.3685 |
| Tellurium | 0.3675 |

The range is 0.3675–0.3830. The most extreme values are:

- **Bismuth** (0.3830) and **Nickel** (0.3819) — highest, deviating ~+0.008 from the approximate center (~0.375)
- **Tellurium** (0.3675) and **Cobalt** (0.3685) — lowest, deviating ~–0.008

**Hydrogen** (gas) is also flagged explicitly: Laroche and Berard's value for hydrogen is described as "rather too small" relative to what the law predicts. The authors attribute this to the gas cooling too rapidly during measurement, introducing systematic error, not to a genuine departure [1819-dulong-petit].

The authors themselves attribute all deviations — including the solid-substance outliers — to "slight errors either in the measurement of the capacities, or in the chemical analyses," noting errors from both sources could compound in the same direction.
