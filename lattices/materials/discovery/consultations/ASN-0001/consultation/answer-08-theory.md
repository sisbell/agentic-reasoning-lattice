## Question 8 [theory]

> How does the theory account for the distinction between sensible heat and latent heat in terms of constituent arrangement?

The corpus does not use the terms "sensible heat" or "latent heat," and does not account for this distinction. The question reaches beyond what the corpus can support.

What the corpus does say about heat and constituent arrangement:

**Three states distinguished by molecular arrangement.** Maxwell opens by classifying bodies by how their molecules move: "The dynamical theory supposes that the molecules of solid bodies oscillate about their positions of equilibrium, but do not travel from one position to another in the body. In fluids the molecules are supposed to be constantly moving into new relative positions... In liquids the molecules are supposed to be always under the action of the forces due to neighbouring molecules throughout their course, but in gases the greater part of the path of each molecule is supposed to be sensibly rectilinear and beyond the sphere of sensible action of the neighbouring molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**Heat as invisible agitation, partitioned between translation and internal motion.** For a single molecule, "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity." The total energy of invisible agitation in unit volume is written $\tfrac{3}{2}\beta p$, where β is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." This quantity "may be called the total heat in the unit of volume of the medium" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**What is absent.** The corpus contains no treatment of phase transitions, no energy associated with rearranging constituents from one state to another, and no accounting for heat absorbed without change of temperature. Maxwell restricts the paper's scope: "I propose in this paper to apply this theory to the explanation of various properties of gases." He acknowledges the extension is open: "A law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

Sensible/latent heat as a distinction in constituent arrangement is therefore outside the corpus. The closest the corpus comes is (i) the qualitative three-state classification by molecular arrangement and (ii) the translation/internal partition of a molecule's energy via β — neither of which is developed into an account of heat absorbed during a change of state.
