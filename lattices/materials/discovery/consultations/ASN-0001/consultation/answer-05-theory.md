## Question 5 [theory]

> What connection does the theory draw between temperature and the mean kinetic energy of constituent motion?

# Temperature and Mean Kinetic Energy of Molecular Motion

The corpus draws a direct quantitative connection, establishing it through the operational criterion of vis viva exchange.

## Temperature as balanced vis viva exchange

[1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases] defines equality of temperature by the cessation of energy transfer between molecular systems:

> "When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same."

And more explicitly in the section on equilibrium of temperature between two gases:

> "Now the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature."

## The quantitative law: equal mean vis viva per molecule

From the analysis of the final distribution of velocities (equation 34), when two gases are at the same temperature:

$$M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$$

The corpus emphasizes the generality of this result:

> "or the mean *vis viva* of a molecule will be the same in each system. This is a very important result in the theory of gases, and it is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules, as are all the other results relating to the final distribution of velocities."

So temperature is identified with the mean translational kinetic energy *per molecule* — a quantity shared across all species in thermal contact, regardless of molecular mass.

## Temperature as proportional to mean square velocity

The corpus makes the scaling explicit. With $\theta$ "the temperature reckoned from absolute zero of a gas thermometer" (equation 101):

$$V^2 = \frac{1}{s}V_0^2\theta$$

— the mean square velocity of a gas is proportional to absolute temperature (inversely to specific gravity $s$). Equivalently (equation 102):

$$p = \tfrac{1}{3}\frac{\rho}{s}\theta V_0^2$$

## Total energy of agitation includes internal motion

The corpus distinguishes translational from internal motion. The full energy of agitation per unit mass is (equation 108):

$$E = \tfrac{3\beta}{2}\frac{p}{\rho}$$

where $\beta$ is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." The internal part is fixed in a constant ratio $(\beta-1)$ to the translational part, brought about, in Maxwell's phrase, "by repeated small exchanges" during encounters. Temperature is governed by the translational portion; the internal energy follows it in fixed proportion.

## Summary

Per the corpus: temperature is that property which equalizes when two molecular systems exchange vis viva without net flow; quantitatively, it is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy per molecule, $\tfrac{1}{2}M\overline{v^2}$, which is the same for molecules of every kind at a given temperature [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].
