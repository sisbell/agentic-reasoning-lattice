## Question 2 [theory]

> How does the internal motion of constituent particles give rise to the thermal state of a body?

# How internal motion gives rise to thermal state

The corpus treats this as the defining commitment of the *dynamical* theory, contrasting with *statical* theories: "those which suppose that the molecules are at rest relative to the body may be called statical theories, and those which suppose the molecules to be in motion, even while the body is apparently at rest, may be called dynamical theories" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Two components of molecular energy

Each molecule carries energy in two parts: "one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity." Internal motion "may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Maxwell writes the total energy per molecule as $\tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2\beta$, where $\beta$ is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation," a ratio fixed on average by the nature of the molecule [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## "Invisible agitation" is the total heat

Decomposing the velocity of each molecule into a mean part $(u,v,w)$ — the "velocities of translation of the medium" — and a fluctuating part $(\xi,\eta,\zeta)$ — the "velocities of agitation of the molecules" — the energy per unit volume splits into three: translation in mass, "agitation of the centres of gravity of the molecules," and "the internal motion of the parts of each molecule" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The latter two combine to

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\beta(\overline{\xi^2}+\overline{\eta^2}+\overline{\zeta^2})\rho = \tfrac{3}{2}\beta p,$$

and Maxwell names this directly: "This energy being in the form of invisible agitation, may be called the total heat in the unit of volume of the medium" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Temperature as the condition of no net transfer

Temperature is defined by the behaviour of this agitation between systems in contact. Collisions cause molecules to exchange *vis viva*; Maxwell shows that "the final result in the case of hard elastic bodies is to cause the average *vis viva* of a molecule to be the same for all the different kinds of molecules," and then states: "When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Later, more generally: "the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Equal-temperature gases therefore satisfy $M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$ (eq. 34), a result "independent of the nature of the action between the molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Pressure as the same motion, observed at a surface

The same agitation produces pressure: momentum carried across an imaginary plane by molecules crossing it gives "a normal pressure $\overline{\xi^2}\rho$" and tangential pressures $\overline{\xi\eta}\rho$, $\overline{\xi\zeta}\rho$ [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. In gases, "we may consider the pressure between the parts of the medium on opposite sides of the plane as entirely due to the constant bombardment kept up between them" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Since "the pressure due to each molecule is proportional to its *vis viva*," pressure and thermal content are two measures of one underlying fact — molecular motion [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Scope the corpus sets

The corpus applies this picture in detail only to gases, where "the greater part of the path of each molecule is supposed to be sensibly rectilinear and beyond the sphere of sensible action of the neighbouring molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. For solids and liquids Maxwell states the same dynamical picture — oscillation about equilibrium in solids, continual rearrangement in fluids — but cautions that "a law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. So for gases the account is quantitative; for other bodies the corpus asserts the principle but not the precise law.
