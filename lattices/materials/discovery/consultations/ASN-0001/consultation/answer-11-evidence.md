## Question 11 [evidence]

> What specific heat values are reported for solid elemental substances in the corpus, and at what temperatures were they measured?

## Specific Heat Values for Solid Elemental Substances

From `[1819-dulong-petit]`, the following specific heats are reported:

| Substance | Specific Heat |
|-----------|--------------|
| Bismuth | 0.0288 |
| Lead | 0.0293 |
| Gold | 0.0298 |
| Platinum | 0.0314 |
| Tin | 0.0514 |
| Silver | 0.0557 |
| Tellurium | 0.0912 |
| Zinc | 0.0927 |
| Copper | 0.0949 |
| Nickel | 0.1035 |
| Iron | 0.1100 |
| Cobalt | 0.1498 |
| Sulphur | 0.1880 |

**Units:** No explicit unit is stated. These are relative specific heats (conventional for the period; water = 1.0 is the implied reference).

**Temperature conditions:** The authors state that "all our experiments were made in an interval of temperature included between 10° and 5° centigrade of excess above the ambient medium." The ambient medium was maintained at the melting-ice temperature (0°C), so measurements were taken over the range of approximately **5–10°C**. No single fixed temperature is given; only this cooling interval is specified.

**Method note:** Substances were measured as fine powders in a small silver vessel under ~2 mm Hg vacuum, using a cooling-rate method. Masses for the densest substances (gold, platinum) did not exceed 30 grams; minimum cooling duration was 15 minutes.

The corpus contains no specific heat values at other temperatures or from other sources.
