## Question 1 [theory]

> What does the theory identify as the microscopic substrate of heat within matter?

### Answer (theory)

The corpus identifies the microscopic substrate of heat as the **invisible agitation of the molecules** — the kinetic energy of their motion relative to the mean motion of the medium, together with internal motion of their parts.

Maxwell writes: "The total energy of the invisible agitation in unit of volume will then be $\tfrac{1}{2}\beta(\overline{\xi^2}+\overline{\eta^2}+\overline{\zeta^2})\rho$, or $\tfrac{3}{2}\beta p$. This energy being in the form of invisible agitation, may be called the total heat in the unit of volume of the medium" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

The energy of the medium decomposes into three parts: "The first term gives the energy due to the motion of translation of the medium in mass, the second that due to the agitation of the centres of gravity of the molecules, and the third that due to the internal motion of the parts of each molecule" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The first is bulk motion; the second and third together constitute heat.

The velocities $\xi, \eta, \zeta$ — the residuals after subtracting the mean $u, v, w$ — are named explicitly: "We shall call the velocities $u$, $v$, $w$ the velocities of translation of the medium, and $\xi$, $\eta$, $\zeta$ the velocities of agitation of the molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

Following Clausius, the ratio of internal to translational energy tends to a fixed value: "$\bar{E} = (\beta - 1)(\overline{\xi^2}+\overline{\eta^2}+\overline{\zeta^2})$" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], so total heat per unit volume is $\tfrac{3}{2}\beta p$.

Temperature is defined by equality of this agitation: "the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Equivalently, molecules of different kinds at the same temperature share the same mean *vis viva*: "$M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

The theory is framed as "dynamical" in explicit contrast to "statical" theories of matter: dynamical theories "suppose the molecules to be in motion, even while the body is apparently at rest" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. In solids the molecules "oscillate about their positions of equilibrium"; in gases "the greater part of the path of each molecule is supposed to be sensibly rectilinear" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. In every case the substrate of heat is molecular motion.

## Question 2 [theory]

> How does the internal motion of constituent particles give rise to the thermal state of a body?

### Answer (theory)

# How internal motion gives rise to thermal state

The corpus treats this as the defining commitment of the *dynamical* theory, contrasting with *statical* theories: "those which suppose that the molecules are at rest relative to the body may be called statical theories, and those which suppose the molecules to be in motion, even while the body is apparently at rest, may be called dynamical theories" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Two components of molecular energy

Each molecule carries energy in two parts: "one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity." Internal motion "may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Maxwell writes the total energy per molecule as $\tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2\beta$, where $\beta$ is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation," a ratio fixed on average by the nature of the molecule [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## "Invisible agitation" is the total heat

Decomposing the velocity of each molecule into a mean part $(u,v,w)$ — the "velocities of translation of the medium" — and a fluctuating part $(\xi,\eta,\zeta)$ — the "velocities of agitation of the molecules" — the energy per unit volume splits into three: translation in mass, "agitation of the centres of gravity of the molecules," and "the internal motion of the parts of each molecule" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The latter two combine to

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\beta(\overline{\xi^2}+\overline{\eta^2}+\overline{\zeta^2})\rho = \tfrac{3}{2}\beta p,$$

and Maxwell names this directly: "This energy being in the form of invisible agitation, may be called the total heat in the unit of volume of the medium" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Temperature as the condition of no net transfer

Temperature is defined by the behaviour of this agitation between systems in contact. Collisions cause molecules to exchange *vis viva*; Maxwell shows that "the final result in the case of hard elastic bodies is to cause the average *vis viva* of a molecule to be the same for all the different kinds of molecules," and then states: "When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Later, more generally: "the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Equal-temperature gases therefore satisfy $M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$ (eq. 34), a result "independent of the nature of the action between the molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Pressure as the same motion, observed at a surface

The same agitation produces pressure: momentum carried across an imaginary plane by molecules crossing it gives "a normal pressure $\overline{\xi^2}\rho$" and tangential pressures $\overline{\xi\eta}\rho$, $\overline{\xi\zeta}\rho$ [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. In gases, "we may consider the pressure between the parts of the medium on opposite sides of the plane as entirely due to the constant bombardment kept up between them" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Since "the pressure due to each molecule is proportional to its *vis viva*," pressure and thermal content are two measures of one underlying fact — molecular motion [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Scope the corpus sets

The corpus applies this picture in detail only to gases, where "the greater part of the path of each molecule is supposed to be sensibly rectilinear and beyond the sphere of sensible action of the neighbouring molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. For solids and liquids Maxwell states the same dynamical picture — oscillation about equilibrium in solids, continual rearrangement in fluids — but cautions that "a law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. So for gases the account is quantitative; for other bodies the corpus asserts the principle but not the precise law.

## Question 3 [theory]

> What role do translational, rotational, and vibrational degrees of freedom play in storing thermal energy?

### Answer (theory)

# Translational vs. Internal Energy Storage in Maxwell 1867

The corpus partitions molecular energy into two parts, not three. It distinguishes **translation of the centre of gravity** from **internal motion relative to the centre of gravity**, and it groups rotation and vibration together under "internal motion" rather than giving them separate roles.

## The two-part decomposition

> "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity. If the body is of invariable form, the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity consist entirely of rotation, but if the parts of the body are not rigidly connected, their motions may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

So rotation appears when the molecule is rigid; oscillations (vibrations) appear additionally when it is not rigid. The corpus treats both as contributors to the same internal bucket.

## The parameter β

Storage across the two buckets is governed by a single ratio:

> "the whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation. … The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

For structureless molecules the internal bucket is empty:

> "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

Following Clausius, the corpus assumes the internal-to-translational ratio relaxes to a fixed value (β − 1):

> "Ē = (β − 1)(ξ² + η² + ζ²)" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

## Total heat in a volume

Summing the three contributions in equation (66):

> "The first term gives the energy due to the motion of translation of the medium in mass, the second that due to the agitation of the centres of gravity of the molecules, and the third that due to the internal motion of the parts of each molecule." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

Only the second and third count as heat; the first is bulk motion. The total invisible agitation per unit volume is ½β(ξ² + η² + ζ²)ρ = (3/2)βp, called "the total heat in the unit of volume" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Pressure depends only on translation

Pressure in the corpus is the transfer of translational momentum across a plane — ρξ̄², ρη̄², ρζ̄² — so internal motion does not enter the pressure directly. That is why, although heat-storage scales with β, the equation of state (eq. 102, p = ⅓(ρ/s)θV₀²) involves only the translational mean-square velocity. Internal motion enters thermodynamics through the specific heats:

> "γ = (2 + 3β)/(3β), whence β = (2/3)·1/(γ−1)" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

So a larger β (more internal storage) raises both specific heats and lowers γ.

## What the corpus does *not* say

The corpus does **not** separate rotation from vibration quantitatively, nor does it assign each degree of freedom an equal share (no equipartition statement per degree of freedom is made in this paper). It lumps rotation and "oscillations of various kinds" into Ē and parameterises the lump with a single β fixed empirically via γ. Anything finer — counting rotational vs vibrational modes, or the high-temperature excitation of vibrations — reaches beyond what this corpus supports.

## Question 4 [theory]

> How does the theory partition energy among the internal modes of a polyatomic constituent?

### Answer (theory)

The corpus partitions molecular energy into two parts only — translation of the centre of gravity and a single lumped "internal" reservoir — and does *not* resolve the internal reservoir into distinct modes.

**The two-part split.** "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity. If the body is of invariable form, the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity consist entirely of rotation, but if the parts of the body are not rigidly connected, their motions may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

**One scalar parameter, not a per-mode partition.** Maxwell rolls rotation and internal oscillation together into a single ratio β: "the whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." Equivalently, the internal part alone is (β−1) times the translational *vis viva*: "$\bar{E} = (\beta - 1)(\overline{\xi^2}+\overline{\eta^2}+\overline{\zeta^2})$." The limiting case — "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" — gives β = 1.

**Equilibration mechanism.** The corpus asserts, without mechanical derivation, that the two reservoirs reach a fixed ratio through many collisions: "in a great many encounters the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1." The assumption is made "with Clausius that the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1)."

**Explicit renunciation of a finer partition.** The corpus acknowledges it cannot divide the internal energy among its sub-modes: "We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules, so that we must content ourselves with the assumption that the value of θ is, on an average, the same as for pure centres of force, and that the final velocities differ from the initial velocities only by quantities which may in each collision be neglected." β is instead fixed empirically from specific heats: "The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them," with γ = (2+3β)/(3β), whence β = (2/3)·1/(γ−1).

**Limit of the corpus.** The corpus therefore does *not* answer — and explicitly says it cannot answer from first principles — how energy is apportioned *within* the internal reservoir (e.g., rotation vs. vibration, or among individual oscillation modes) of a polyatomic constituent. It delivers only a single translation-to-internal ratio, phenomenologically calibrated.

## Question 5 [theory]

> What connection does the theory draw between temperature and the mean kinetic energy of constituent motion?

### Answer (theory)

# Temperature and Mean Kinetic Energy of Molecular Motion

The corpus draws a direct quantitative connection, establishing it through the operational criterion of vis viva exchange.

## Temperature as balanced vis viva exchange

[1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases] defines equality of temperature by the cessation of energy transfer between molecular systems:

> "When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same."

And more explicitly in the section on equilibrium of temperature between two gases:

> "Now the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature."

## The quantitative law: equal mean vis viva per molecule

From the analysis of the final distribution of velocities (equation 34), when two gases are at the same temperature:

$$M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$$

The corpus emphasizes the generality of this result:

> "or the mean *vis viva* of a molecule will be the same in each system. This is a very important result in the theory of gases, and it is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules, as are all the other results relating to the final distribution of velocities."

So temperature is identified with the mean translational kinetic energy *per molecule* — a quantity shared across all species in thermal contact, regardless of molecular mass.

## Temperature as proportional to mean square velocity

The corpus makes the scaling explicit. With $\theta$ "the temperature reckoned from absolute zero of a gas thermometer" (equation 101):

$$V^2 = \frac{1}{s}V_0^2\theta$$

— the mean square velocity of a gas is proportional to absolute temperature (inversely to specific gravity $s$). Equivalently (equation 102):

$$p = \tfrac{1}{3}\frac{\rho}{s}\theta V_0^2$$

## Total energy of agitation includes internal motion

The corpus distinguishes translational from internal motion. The full energy of agitation per unit mass is (equation 108):

$$E = \tfrac{3\beta}{2}\frac{p}{\rho}$$

where $\beta$ is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." The internal part is fixed in a constant ratio $(\beta-1)$ to the translational part, brought about, in Maxwell's phrase, "by repeated small exchanges" during encounters. Temperature is governed by the translational portion; the internal energy follows it in fixed proportion.

## Summary

Per the corpus: temperature is that property which equalizes when two molecular systems exchange vis viva without net flow; quantitatively, it is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy per molecule, $\tfrac{1}{2}M\overline{v^2}$, which is the same for molecules of every kind at a given temperature [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Question 6 [theory]

> How do intermolecular forces between constituents modify the purely kinetic account of heat?

### Answer (theory)

# How intermolecular forces modify the purely kinetic account of heat

A gas of non-interacting molecules in a box would, by Maxwell's own demonstration, behave "not like a fluid, but like an elastic solid" with rigidity equal to the pressure $p$ [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Forces between molecules convert this elastic solid into a fluid and reshape the account of heat in several distinct ways.

**1. Forces equalize pressure; their finite rate gives viscosity.** "To account for the observed equality of pressure in all directions, we must suppose some cause equalizing the motion in all directions. This we find in the deflection of the path of one particle by another when they come near one another." Because the equalization is rapid but not instantaneous, "there remains a certain inequality of pressure which constitutes the phenomenon of viscosity" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The instantaneous rigidity is $p$, the time of relaxation $T$, and the coefficient of viscosity $\mu = pT$.

**2. The specific law of force enters through the collision frequency.** Maxwell replaces hard elastic spheres by centers of force — "small bodies or groups of smaller molecules repelling one another" — and selects the inverse fifth-power repulsion because "if the force is inversely as the fifth power of the distance, the number of deflections of a given kind will be independent of the velocity," making $\mu$ independent of density and proportional to absolute temperature [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**3. Mutual action enforces equipartition across species (Law of Equivalent Volumes).** The forces "communicate motion from the one kind to the other," driving $M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$. This result is "independent of the nature of the action between the molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases] — any force would do; what matters is that some exists.

**4. Mutual action partitions translational and internal energy.** For molecules with rotation or internal vibration, "the mutual interference of the molecules in their courses will cause their energy of motion to be distributed in a certain ratio between that due to the motion of the centre of gravity and that due to the rotation, or other internal motion." The ratio settles at a definite value, and the total invisible agitation per unit volume becomes $\tfrac{3}{2}\beta p$ rather than $\tfrac{3}{2}p$; the factor $\beta$ fixes the specific-heat ratio $\gamma = (2+3\beta)/(3\beta)$ [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**5. But forces do *not* contribute directly to pressure in gases.** The mechanical action across a plane "consists partly of the momentum thus transferred, and partly of the direct attractions or repulsions between molecules on opposite sides of the plane. The latter part of the action must be very small in gases, so that we may consider the pressure … as entirely due to the constant bombardment" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Forces act on trajectories, not on the pressure tally itself.

**6. A coincidence Maxwell flags.** The same rigidity and ratio of elasticities arise in a medium of freely moving molecules as in a statical medium held by central forces. "The coincidence of the properties of a medium in which the molecules are held in equilibrium by attractions and repulsions, and those of a medium in which the molecules move in straight lines without acting on each other at all, deserves notice from those who speculate on theories of physics. The fluidity of our medium is therefore due to the mutual action of the molecules, causing them to be deflected from their paths" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**What the corpus leaves open.** Maxwell is explicit that the detailed exchange between translational and internal energy within a single encounter is beyond reach: "We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules," so he assumes the average deflection angle is that of pure centers of force and that final ratios are set by many small exchanges [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The value of $\beta$ itself is not derived; it is to be fixed from measured specific heats or their ratio.

## Question 7 [theory]

> What does the theory predict about the heat capacity of a body from the number of active degrees of freedom of its constituents?

### Answer (theory)

## Heat capacity and internal energy modes

The corpus does **not** use the phrase "degrees of freedom." Instead, it parameterizes all internal energy storage by a single lumped ratio β.

### Energy partition

Molecular energy is split into translation of the centre of gravity and internal motion relative to it:

> "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity. If the body is of invariable form, the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity consist entirely of rotation, but if the parts of the body are not rigidly connected, their motions may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

### The parameter β

The total energy per molecule is written ½Mv²β, "where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." For pure centres of force there is no rotation and β = 1; otherwise β > 1. Following Clausius, the corpus assumes the "ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1)" so that, except in violent disturbances, the partition is preserved [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases, eq. 67].

### Specific heats

Taking the total agitational energy per unit volume as ½β(ξ̄² + η̄² + ζ̄²)ρ = ³⁄₂ βp (eqs. 68–69), the corpus derives:

- Specific heat at constant volume: (3β/2) · p/(ρθ) [eq. 110]
- Specific heat at constant pressure: (2 + 3β)/2 · p/(ρθ) [eq. 112]
- Ratio γ ≡ cp/cv = (2 + 3β)/(3β), whence β = (2/3)·1/(γ − 1) [eqs. 113–114]

### What the theory predicts

Heat capacity scales linearly with β. More active internal modes (rotation, oscillation) raise β above 1, lowering γ and raising both specific heats together with their difference held fixed in the ratio above. The corpus is explicit that β is an empirical input:

> "The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

The corpus does not derive β from molecular structure or count internal modes — it reads β off the measured γ. It also notes (citing Rankine and Regnault) that applying these formulae to air reproduces the measured specific heat [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

### What the corpus does not say

The corpus does not state an equipartition count of the form "½kT per quadratic mode" nor predict specific integer values of β for particular molecular geometries. Any such enumeration is beyond what this corpus supports.

## Question 8 [theory]

> How does the theory account for the distinction between sensible heat and latent heat in terms of constituent arrangement?

### Answer (theory)

The corpus does not use the terms "sensible heat" or "latent heat," and does not account for this distinction. The question reaches beyond what the corpus can support.

What the corpus does say about heat and constituent arrangement:

**Three states distinguished by molecular arrangement.** Maxwell opens by classifying bodies by how their molecules move: "The dynamical theory supposes that the molecules of solid bodies oscillate about their positions of equilibrium, but do not travel from one position to another in the body. In fluids the molecules are supposed to be constantly moving into new relative positions... In liquids the molecules are supposed to be always under the action of the forces due to neighbouring molecules throughout their course, but in gases the greater part of the path of each molecule is supposed to be sensibly rectilinear and beyond the sphere of sensible action of the neighbouring molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**Heat as invisible agitation, partitioned between translation and internal motion.** For a single molecule, "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity." The total energy of invisible agitation in unit volume is written $\tfrac{3}{2}\beta p$, where β is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." This quantity "may be called the total heat in the unit of volume of the medium" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**What is absent.** The corpus contains no treatment of phase transitions, no energy associated with rearranging constituents from one state to another, and no accounting for heat absorbed without change of temperature. Maxwell restricts the paper's scope: "I propose in this paper to apply this theory to the explanation of various properties of gases." He acknowledges the extension is open: "A law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

Sensible/latent heat as a distinction in constituent arrangement is therefore outside the corpus. The closest the corpus comes is (i) the qualitative three-state classification by molecular arrangement and (ii) the translation/internal partition of a molecule's energy via β — neither of which is developed into an account of heat absorbed during a change of state.

## Question 9 [theory]

> What does the theory say about the transfer of thermal energy through collisions between constituents?

### Answer (theory)

# Transfer of Thermal Energy Through Collisions Between Constituents

The corpus treats thermal-energy transfer between different molecular constituents as a direct consequence of their **encounters** (collisions), and distinguishes it from the bulk motion of the gas.

## Mechanism and endpoint

Maxwell states the basic mechanism directly:

> "The effect of the mutual action of the molecules is not only to equalize the pressure in all directions, but, when molecules of different kinds are present, to communicate motion from the one kind to the other. I formerly showed that the final result in the case of hard elastic bodies is to cause the average *vis viva* of a molecule to be the same for all the different kinds of molecules." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

The endpoint is formalized as the equipartition result (eq. 34):

$$M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$$

which Maxwell emphasizes "is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules, as are all the other results relating to the final distribution of velocities." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

## Definition of equal temperature

Thermal equilibrium between constituents is *defined* by zero net exchange of vis viva through collisions:

> "When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

## Rate of approach

In the "Equilibrium of Temperature between two Gases" section, Maxwell writes the equation governing the mutual transfer (eq. 94):

$$\tfrac{1}{2}\rho_1\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\beta_1(\overline{\xi_1^2}+\overline{\eta_1^2}+\overline{\zeta_1^2}) = \frac{k\rho_1\rho_2 A_1}{M_1+M_2}\left\{M_2(\overline{\xi_2^2}+\overline{\eta_2^2}+\overline{\zeta_2^2}) - M_1(\overline{\xi_1^2}+\overline{\eta_1^2}+\overline{\zeta_1^2})\right\}$$

Defining $Q_i = \tfrac{M_i}{M_1+M_2}(\overline{\xi_i^2}+\overline{\eta_i^2}+\overline{\zeta_i^2})$, the difference decays exponentially (eqs. 96–97):

$$Q_2-Q_1 = Ce^{-nt}, \quad n = \frac{2kA_1}{M_1+M_2}(M_2\rho_2\beta_2+M_1\rho_1\beta_1)\frac{1}{\beta_1\beta_2}$$

> "If, therefore, the gases are in contact and undisturbed, $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ will rapidly become equal. Now the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

The rate constant depends on $k$ (the coefficient of mutual interference between the two kinds), on $A_1$ (the collision-integral constant), and on the densities and masses. Under the $n=5$ force law used throughout, $V$ drops out, so the rate is independent of the relative velocity of the molecules.

## Internal vs. translational energy

Collisions also couple translation to internal motion. Following Clausius:

> "the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to $\beta - 1$." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

Thus $\bar{E} = (\beta-1)(\overline{\xi^2}+\overline{\eta^2}+\overline{\zeta^2})$ (eq. 67), and the total invisible agitation per unit volume is $\tfrac{3}{2}\beta p$ (eq. 69).

## Collision-heating in diffusion

When the two constituents move relative to one another, the encounters contribute a positive heating term. From equation (117):

$$\ldots = k\rho_1\rho_2 A_1\left\{(u_1-u_2)^2+(v_1-v_2)^2+(w_1-w_2)^2\right\}$$

Maxwell identifies "the thermal effects of diffusion, and the communication of heat from one gas to the other" as what these right-hand terms represent [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. He further notes that under constant-volume interdiffusion "the heat due to the mutual action of the gases in diffusion will be exactly neutralized by the cooling of each gas as it expands."

## Consequence: Law of Equivalent Volumes

Because pressure per molecule is proportional to its vis viva, and collisions equalize mean vis viva across kinds at the same temperature:

> "equal volumes of different gases at equal pressures and temperatures contain equal numbers of molecules." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

Maxwell flags this as a "necessary result of the Dynamical Theory of Gases; and it is so, whatever theory we adopt as to the nature of the action between the individual molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Scope note

The corpus treats inter-constituent transfer via binary encounters under the assumption that "we may neglect those cases in which three or more molecules are within each other's spheres of action at the same instant" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The detailed transfer rates (eqs. 36–39) are computed specifically for the inverse-fifth-power repulsion, which is "the only case which is consistent with the laws of viscosity of gases" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]; the equipartition endpoint itself, however, the corpus states does not depend on that choice.

## Question 10 [theory]

> How does the structural phase of matter — its degree of internal order or disorder — constrain the distribution of thermal energy among its constituents?

### Answer (theory)

The corpus distinguishes three structural phases by the character of molecular motion, and gives a quantitative answer for the gas phase only.

**Phase-dependent motion.** The dynamical theory supposes that "the molecules of solid bodies oscillate about their positions of equilibrium, but do not travel from one position to another in the body. In fluids the molecules are supposed to be constantly moving into new relative positions… In liquids the molecules are supposed to be always under the action of the forces due to neighbouring molecules throughout their course, but in gases the greater part of the path of each molecule is supposed to be sensibly rectilinear and beyond the sphere of sensible action of the neighbouring molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**Energy partition in the gas phase.** Each molecule's energy is written as $\tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2\beta$, where β is "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." For pure centres of force "there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation"; for compound molecules, translation and internal motion "arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio… of 1 to β − 1." Across two species in contact, equation (34) gives $M_1\overline{v_1^2}=M_2\overline{v_2^2}$ — the mean vis viva of translation per molecule is equal, and this result "is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules." The final velocity distribution (eq. 26) is Gaussian in each component, with the cross-species constraint $M_1\alpha^2 = M_2\beta^2$ (eq. 25).

**Phase-dependent relaxation of form-elasticity.** The corpus treats the structural question of "order" through the coefficient of elasticity E and the time of relaxation T, where the coefficient of viscosity is ET. "In mobile fluids T is a very small fraction of a second, and E is not easily determined experimentally. In viscous solids T may be several hours or days, and then E is easily measured." A confined gas has instantaneous rigidity equal to p, but encounters continually relax it, so "this rigidity… cannot be directly observed."

**Why a purely statical picture fails.** For molecules held rigidly in equilibrium positions, "the forces called into play by a small change of form must always bear a fixed proportion to those excited by a small change of volume. Now we know that in fluids the elasticity of form is evanescent, while that of volume is considerable. Hence such theories will not apply to fluids." Even in solids the observed ratio of form to volume elasticity "is smaller in proportion… than the theory gives," forcing abandonment of the statical picture.

**What the corpus declines to state.** For phases other than gas, Maxwell is explicit about the limit of what he can assert: "A law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**Summary of what the corpus supports, precisely.** (i) In gases, equality of translational vis viva per molecule across species at a common temperature, independent of the force law. (ii) In gases, a β-ratio partition between translational and internal degrees, whose value depends on molecular structure and is fixed by the ratio of specific heats (eq. 114). (iii) Phase-dependent relaxation times T that govern how quickly anisotropic stress dissolves into isotropic pressure. The question "how does structural phase constrain thermal-energy distribution" thus receives a complete answer within the corpus only for gases; for liquids and solids the corpus asserts by analogy and defers the precise law.

**2. Method of Mixture (mixing with water)**
Described as potentially exact but requiring large sample masses. The authors did not use it as their primary method. It is cited as a validation cross-check: "having compared the specific heats thus obtained for the worst conductors with those given by the method of mixture, or by the calorimeter." No specific substances are named for this comparison.

**3. Calorimeter (ice calorimeter, Lavoisier & Laplace)**
Referenced as the source of the few accurate prior measurements: "we except those of Lavoisier and Laplace (unfortunately very few)." No specific substances are identified for these measurements in the text.

**4. Direct measurement of elastic fluids (Laroche & Berard)**
A separate experimental series, not by Dulong & Petit. Applied to: oxygen, azotic gas (nitrogen), and hydrogen. Numerical results are referenced qualitatively (oxygen and nitrogen said to agree with the law; hydrogen noted as slightly low, attributed to rapid cooling error). No specific heat values are quoted for these gases in the paper.

### Summary

The cooling-curve method is the only one for which specific substances and numerical values are given [1819-dulong-petit]. The method of mixture and calorimeter are cited as validation references and prior-work benchmarks, with no substance-level data reported for them. Gas measurements (Laroche & Berard) are cited by name of gas but without quoted numerical values.

> Do any substances in the data deviate from otherwise regular heat-capacity patterns, and what are the outliers?

### Answer (evidence)

## Deviations from the equal-atomic-heat-capacity pattern

The paper's own test for regularity is the product (atomic weight × specific heat), which the authors claim should be constant across all elements. The 13-substance table yields these products [1819-dulong-petit]:

| Substance | Product |
|-----------|---------|

| Nickel | 0.3819 |
| Lead | 0.3794 |

| Tin | 0.3779 |
| Silver | 0.3759 |

| Platinum | 0.3740 |
| Gold | 0.3704 |
| Cobalt | 0.3685 |
| Tellurium | 0.3675 |

The range is 0.3675–0.3830. The most extreme values are:

- **Bismuth** (0.3830) and **Nickel** (0.3819) — highest, deviating ~+0.008 from the approximate center (~0.375)
- **Tellurium** (0.3675) and **Cobalt** (0.3685) — lowest, deviating ~–0.008

**Hydrogen** (gas) is also flagged explicitly: Laroche and Berard's value for hydrogen is described as "rather too small" relative to what the law predicts. The authors attribute this to the gas cooling too rapidly during measurement, introducing systematic error, not to a genuine departure [1819-dulong-petit].

The authors themselves attribute all deviations — including the solid-substance outliers — to "slight errors either in the measurement of the capacities, or in the chemical analyses," noting errors from both sources could compound in the same direction.

## Question 20 [evidence]

No. The corpus does not report heat capacities for the same substance across different physical states.
