## Question 10 [theory]

> What must hold about β for a system of perfectly smooth elastic spheres, and what does this reveal about the constitution such a model assumes?

**β for perfectly smooth elastic spheres**

Maxwell defines β as "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The corpus does not name "perfectly smooth elastic spheres" as a distinct case, but the relevant reasoning is supplied for an adjacent one:

> "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

For smooth spheres the same conclusion follows by inference: collisions transmit force only along the line of centres, so no torque acts, and rotational energy cannot be communicated through encounters. Whatever rotation a sphere happens to carry is decoupled from the translational thermal equilibration described by equations (4)–(7) and (36)–(43). Under Maxwell's thermalized ratio, β = 1.

**Consequence via specific heats**

From the corpus,

> "γ = (2+3β)/(3β), whence β = (2/3)(1/(γ−1))" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

β = 1 forces γ = 5/3.

**What this reveals about the assumed constitution**

The smooth-elastic-sphere model tacitly assumes a molecule with no internal degree of freedom that can thermalize with translation — mechanically indistinguishable from a point, or from a body whose internal motions are inaccessible to collision. Maxwell is explicit that real molecules may instead be "systems of several such centres of force, bound together by their mutual actions" or "small solid bodies of a determinate form" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], in which case "the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], so β > 1.

He notes that β is fixed empirically: "The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. His datum "For air we have γ = 1.409" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases] yields β ≈ 1.63, and γ = 1.27 for carbonic acid yields β ≈ 2.47 — both incompatible with β = 1. The smooth-sphere constitution is therefore a limiting fiction that real gases violate: they carry invisible agitation beyond translation, which a smooth sphere has no mechanism to receive or surrender.
