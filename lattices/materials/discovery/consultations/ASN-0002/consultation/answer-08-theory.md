## Question 8 [theory]

> How does the theory treat the equilibrium condition between the translational and internal vis viva of a single species of molecule?

# Equilibrium between translational and internal vis viva in a single species

The corpus does not *derive* this equilibrium; it posits it, following Clausius, and attaches an empirical handle to it through the specific heats.

## The posited ratio

Maxwell introduces β as the ratio of total molecular energy to energy of translation:

> "in all other cases the whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation. The ratio β will be different for every molecule, and will be different for the same molecule after every encounter with another molecule, but it will have an average value depending on the nature of the molecules, as has been shown by Clausius. The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

So the equilibrium condition is that the *mean* ratio β settles to a fixed value characteristic of the molecular species, even though the ratio fluctuates molecule-by-molecule and encounter-by-encounter.

## The mechanism: repeated small exchanges

The corpus is explicit that the exact internal dynamics of an encounter cannot be solved, so the approach to equilibrium is assumed, not computed:

> "We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules, so that we must content ourselves with the assumption that the value of θ is, on an average, the same as for pure centres of force, and that the final velocities differ from the initial velocities only by quantities which may in each collision be neglected, although in a great many encounters the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

A single collision is treated as elastic for the purposes of the deflection integral; the translational–internal equilibration is treated as a slow drift accomplished by many such collisions.

## The equilibrium relation used downstream

Once equilibrium is invoked, the mean internal energy per molecule is tied rigidly to the translational agitation:

> "If we assume with Clausius that the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1), we may conclude that, except in very violent disturbances, this ratio is always preserved, so that
> Ē = (β − 1)(ξ̄² + η̄² + ζ̄²)." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

This lets the total heat per unit volume be written

> "½β(ξ̄² + η̄² + ζ̄²)ρ, or ³⁄₂ βp" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

and β is then fixed from experiment via

> "γ = (2 + 3β)/(3β), whence β = ⅔ · 1/(γ−1)." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

## What the corpus does *not* do

- It does not derive β from a model of internal molecular structure; pure centres of force are noted as a limiting case with no rotational energy at all.
- It does not give a relaxation time for the translational–internal equilibration analogous to the relaxation time T for shear stress; the exchange is simply assumed to be rapid compared with "violent disturbances."
- For a pure centre of force, the treatment collapses: "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The equilibrium question only arises for molecules with internal degrees of freedom.

In short: the corpus treats the translational/internal equilibrium as a Clausius-style postulate — a stable mean ratio 1 : (β − 1) reached by many small collisional exchanges, with β taken from measured specific-heat ratios rather than computed from first principles.
