## Question 1 [theory]

> What does the theory commit to regarding the nature of heat as a state of motion of molecules rather than a material substance?

### Answer (theory)

The theory commits heat to being a **state of molecular motion** — what the paper calls "invisible agitation" — not a material substance. The commitments are layered:

**1. Heat is identified with the energy of molecular motion.** After deriving the total energy of agitation per unit volume, the paper states: *"This energy being in the form of invisible agitation, may be called the total heat in the unit of volume of the medium"* `[1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]`. The energy decomposes into translation of centres of gravity plus internal motion, in ratio 1 to β−1 (following Clausius).

**2. Temperature is a condition of that motion.** *"When the flow of vis viva from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same"*; and *"the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature."* Equal temperature means equal mean vis viva per molecule: *"M₁v̄₁² = M₂v̄₂², or the mean vis viva of a molecule will be the same in each system. This is a very important result in the theory of gases, and it is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules"* (eqn 34).

**3. Conduction of heat is transport of molecular energy across a plane**, not passage of a fluid. The section "Transference of Energy across a Plane — Conduction of Heat" derives the flux as $\tfrac{1}{2}\beta(\overline{\xi^3}+\overline{\xi\eta^2}+\overline{\xi\zeta^2})\rho$ (eqn 71).

**4. Specific heats emerge from motion accounting.** With β = (2/3)/(γ−1) (eqn 114), the paper derives cv and cp purely from the kinetic decomposition, noting Rankine's calculated value *"agree[s] with the value afterwards determined experimentally by M. Regnault."*

**5. The dynamical commitment is adopted because the statical alternative fails.** Statical theories (molecules at rest, held by central forces) predict a fixed ratio of form-elasticity to volume-elasticity contradicted by fluids ("the elasticity of form is evanescent") and even by solids; *"we are forced to give up the theory of molecules whose displacements are functions of their coordinates when at rest."* The dynamical theory *"is not open to these objections."* Fluidity itself is a motion effect: *"The fluidity of our medium is therefore due to the mutual action of the molecules, causing them to be deflected from their paths."*

**6. Second-law consistency is invoked to discipline the theory.** In the Dec 17, 1866 addition, Maxwell notes that any height-dependence of equilibrium temperature would permit a perpetual-motion cycle *"in contradiction to the second law of thermodynamics"* — and treats the corrected uniform-temperature result *"as in some measure a confirmation of the law of distribution of velocities."*

**What the theory does not commit to.** It declines to fix the internal constitution of molecules — they may be points, centres of force, groups, or (if necessary) small solid bodies — and only assumes internal energy stands in a definite ratio β−1 to translational energy: *"We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules."*

## Question 2 [theory]

> What does the theory predict about the relationship between temperature and the translational vis viva of molecules constituting a body?

### Answer (theory)

# Temperature and Translational Vis Viva in Maxwell's Theory

The corpus defines temperature equality in terms of equal mean translational vis viva per molecule, and derives from the equilibrium distribution that mean square velocity varies directly with absolute temperature.

## Temperature as equality of translational vis viva

Maxwell identifies temperature with the condition under which net exchange of translational energy ceases between two systems of molecules:

> "When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

The equilibrium condition derived from the final distribution of velocities (eq. 34) gives:

$$M_1 \overline{v_1^2} = M_2 \overline{v_2^2}$$

> "or the mean *vis viva* of a molecule will be the same in each system. This is a very important result in the theory of gases, and it is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

The later diffusion-equilibrium analysis (eqs. 94–99) restates this: at the same temperature, $Q_1 = Q_2$, where $Q_i = \frac{M_i}{M_1+M_2}(\overline{\xi_i^2}+\overline{\eta_i^2}+\overline{\zeta_i^2})$ — i.e. translational vis viva per molecule (up to a common factor) is equalized.

## Mean square velocity scales linearly with absolute temperature

Once temperature is tied to translational vis viva, the corpus gives the scaling explicitly. For any gas of specific gravity $s$ referred to hydrogen, with $\theta$ measured from absolute zero of the gas thermometer:

$$V^2 = \frac{1}{s} V_0^2 \theta \quad \text{(eq. 101)}$$

> "Its mean square of velocity, $V^2 = \frac{1}{s}V_0^2\theta$." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

Equivalently, from eq. 105 (adiabatic relation) and the pressure expression $p = \tfrac{1}{3}(\rho/s)\theta V_0^2$ (eq. 102), $p/\rho \propto \theta$, and since $\overline{\xi^2} + \overline{\eta^2} + \overline{\zeta^2} = 3p/\rho$, the mean translational vis viva per unit mass is proportional to $\theta$.

## What the corpus states vs. what it infers

- **Stated as result of the theory:** temperature equality ⇔ equality of mean translational vis viva per molecule; this holds whatever the law of intermolecular force.
- **Stated as derivation:** $V^2 \propto \theta$ follows once the absolute scale $\theta$ is introduced through the gas thermometer.
- **Note on scope:** the corpus distinguishes translational vis viva from total molecular energy. The total energy per molecule is $\tfrac{1}{2} M v^2 \beta$, where $\beta$ accounts for internal (rotational/vibrational) modes (eq. 67). Temperature is tied to the translational part; the internal part is assumed to reach a fixed ratio $(\beta-1)$ to translation through repeated encounters, following Clausius.
- **Limits the corpus acknowledges:** the law-of-temperature-uniformity-with-height argument (addition of Dec. 17, 1866) leans on the second law of thermodynamics and on $\overline{\xi^4} = 3\,\overline{\xi^2}\cdot\overline{\xi^2}$ from the equilibrium distribution; Maxwell treats the consistency as "in some measure a confirmation of the law of distribution of velocities." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

## Summary

The theory predicts that the mean translational vis viva per molecule is the measure of temperature: two bodies are at the same temperature iff $M \overline{v^2}$ is equal across their molecules, and the mean square velocity of molecules in a given gas is directly proportional to absolute temperature $\theta$. The first of these is independent of the law of intermolecular force; the second follows once the equilibrium (Maxwellian) distribution is established.

## Question 3 [theory]

> How does the theory characterize the internal constitution of a molecule such that vis viva may reside in modes beyond translational motion?

### Answer (theory)

The theory admits several candidate constitutions for a molecule, and what non-translational modes (if any) exist depends on which constitution is assumed.

**Candidate constitutions.** Molecules "may be mere points, or pure centres of force endowed with inertia, or the capacity of performing work while losing velocity. They may be systems of several such centres of force, bound together by their mutual actions… Finally, if necessary, we may suppose them to be small solid bodies of a determinate form; but in this case we must assume a new set of forces binding the parts of these small bodies together, and so introduce a molecular theory of the second order" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Maxwell also notes that the extension and impenetrability of sensible bodies "have no application to the theory of molecules."

**Modes permitted by each structure.** "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity. If the body is of invariable form, the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity consist entirely of rotation, but if the parts of the body are not rigidly connected, their motions may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body." Crucially, "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation."

**Summary in the ratio β.** "In all other cases the whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation." β varies molecule-to-molecule and encounter-to-encounter, "but it will have an average value depending on the nature of the molecules, as has been shown by Clausius," and "can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them."

**Agnosticism about the internal form.** At the medium level the energy is written ½M{(u+ξ)² + …} + ½EM, where "½EM is the internal part of the energy of the molecule, the form of which is at present unknown." Following Clausius, Maxwell *assumes* Ē = (β−1)(ξ²+η²+ζ²), so that, "except in very violent disturbances, this ratio is always preserved."

**Collision treatment.** "If we suppose the molecules to be bodies, or systems of bodies, capable of rotation, internal vibration, or any form of energy other than simple motion of translation, these results will be modified. The value of θ and the final velocities of the molecules will depend on the amount of internal energy in each molecule before the encounter, and on the particular form of that energy at every instant during the mutual action. We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules," so Maxwell assumes θ averages to its pure-centres-of-force value, and that translational and internal energies reach "by repeated small exchanges, a final ratio… of 1 to β − 1."

In short: the corpus characterises internal constitution permissively (point, centre-of-force, group, or rigid body with second-order internal forces), tracks only a single aggregate ratio β, and treats the detailed *form* of the internal energy as outside the reach of the theory.

## Question 4 [theory]

> What must hold about the ratio β between total vis viva and translational vis viva for bodies whose molecules possess internal structure?

### Answer (theory)

For molecules with internal structure, the corpus requires several conditions on β:

**1. β > 1 (strict inequality).** The corpus defines β as the ratio of total to translational energy: "the whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. For pure centres of force "there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" — i.e. β = 1. Any molecule capable of "rotation, internal vibration, or any form of energy other than simple motion of translation" must therefore have β > 1.

**2. β tends to a definite mean value set by the molecule's nature.** Although β "will be different for every molecule, and will be different for the same molecule after every encounter," it "will have an average value depending on the nature of the molecules, as has been shown by Clausius" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Equivalently, the internal and translational energies approach "a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1."

**3. That ratio is preserved outside violent disturbances.** "If we assume with Clausius that the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1), we may conclude that, except in very violent disturbances, this ratio is always preserved, so that Ē = (β − 1)(ξ̄² + η̄² + ζ̄²)" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**4. β is fixed by the measured specific-heat ratio.** "The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them." The corpus derives γ = (2 + 3β)/(3β), and hence β = (2/3)·1/(γ − 1) [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases, eqs. (113)–(114)]. Since γ > 1 for real gases, this again forces β > 1; γ = 5/3 recovers β = 1 (no internal energy).

What the corpus does *not* specify is the mechanism that fixes β for a given molecule — Maxwell explicitly says "We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules," and simply assumes the Clausius result.

## Question 5 [theory]

> What does elastic collision require of the constituent bodies, and what does this commitment say about their fundamental nature?

### Answer (theory)

# What elastic collision requires, and what that commitment says about the molecule

## The commitment as Herapath made it

Treating collision as the rebound of "perfectly hard bodies" — what Maxwell calls the hypothesis of "elastic spheres of definite radius" — requires the constituents to have definite extension, rigidity, and impenetrability. Maxwell identifies this commitment as decisively broken on two counts.

First, it violates the relativity of motion. Herapath's "theory of the collisions of perfectly hard bodies, such as he supposes the molecules to be, is faulty, inasmuch as it makes the result of impact depend on the absolute motion of the bodies, so that by experiments on such hard bodies (if we could get them) we might determine the absolute direction and velocity of the motion of the earth" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. A collision law that lets absolute motion leak out through experiment cannot be correct.

Second, it gives the wrong temperature dependence for viscosity. Hard elastic molecules "would make it vary as the square root of the absolute temperature"; experiment shows viscosity proportional to the absolute temperature, which "requires us to abandon this hypothesis ... and to adopt the hypothesis of a repulsive force inversely as the fifth power of the distance between the molecules, which is the only law of force which gives the observed result" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## What the hard-sphere commitment implicitly demands

If one insists on extended solid bodies as molecules, Maxwell warns of a hidden cost: "if necessary, we may suppose them to be small solid bodies of a determinate form; but in this case we must assume a new set of forces binding the parts of these small bodies together, and so introduce a molecular theory of the second order" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Rigidity is not free — it presupposes an inner binding law, pushing the problem down a level.

## What this says about the fundamental nature of the molecule

Maxwell draws the conclusion explicitly: the macroscopic attributes we associate with "bodies" need not attach to molecules at all. "The doctrines that all matter is extended, and that no two portions of matter can coincide in the same place, being deductions from our experiments with bodies sensible to us, have no application to the theory of molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

Accordingly, the molecule is redescribed as whatever "moves about as a single body": "These molecules may be mere points, or pure centres of force endowed with inertia, or the capacity of performing work while losing velocity. They may be systems of several such centres of force, bound together by their mutual actions, and in this case the different centres may either be separated, so as to form a group of points, or they may be actually coincident, so as to form one point" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

The "collision," on this reading, is not an impact of surfaces but a deflection in a central orbit: "If we regard the molecules as simple centres of force, then each molecule will describe a plane curve about this centre of gravity, and the two curves will be similar to each other and symmetrical with respect to the line of apses" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The encounter is specified by the relative velocity $V$, the offset $b$, and the plane angle $\varphi$; the outcome is the deflection $2\theta$, determined once the law of force is given.

Maxwell underscores how little the observable consequences constrain the underlying picture: "The same result and the same ratio of the elasticities would be obtained if we supposed the molecules to be at rest, and to act on one another with forces depending on the distance, as in the statical molecular theory of elasticity. The coincidence of the properties of a medium in which the molecules are held in equilibrium by attractions and repulsions, and those of a medium in which the molecules move in straight lines without acting on each other at all, deserves notice from those who speculate on theories of physics" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Summary of the commitment

In the corpus, the demand for literal elastic collision commits one to extended, impenetrable, rigid bodies — and this commitment (i) contradicts the relativity of motion, (ii) contradicts the measured temperature law of viscosity, and (iii) requires an unexplained inner binding theory. Maxwell's response is to drop the commitment: the molecule's fundamental nature is that of a centre (or system of centres) of force endowed with inertia, and "collision" is a deflection governed by a continuous law of force — for the gases treated here, a repulsion inversely as the fifth power of distance.

## Question 6 [theory]

> How does the theory distinguish between the vis viva of molecular translation and the vis viva associated with motion internal to each molecule at equilibrium?

### Answer (theory)

The corpus distinguishes the two components through a decomposition of molecular energy and a single ratio β.

**Two components of molecular energy.** "The actual energy of a moving body consists of two parts, one due to the motion of its centre of gravity, and the other due to the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity. If the body is of invariable form, the motions of its parts relative to the centre of gravity consist entirely of rotation, but if the parts of the body are not rigidly connected, their motions may consist of oscillations of various kinds, as well as rotation of the whole body" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

**The ratio β.** "The whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation. The ratio β will be different for every molecule, and will be different for the same molecule after every encounter with another molecule, but it will have an average value depending on the nature of the molecules, as has been shown by Clausius" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. For pure centres of force, "there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation."

**Equilibrium assumption.** The translational and internal shares settle to a fixed proportion through encounters: "in a great many encounters the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Equivalently: "If we assume with Clausius that the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1) … this ratio is always preserved, so that Ē = (β − 1)(ξ² + η² + ζ²)" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The total invisible-agitation energy per unit volume is then ½β(ξ² + η² + ζ²)ρ = (3/2)βp.

**Only translation equalizes between species.** Temperature equality between two gases is defined by the flow of vis viva of translation being zero. The derivation in the "Law of Equivalent Volumes" section yields M₁(ξ₁² + η₁² + ζ₁²) = M₂(ξ₂² + η₂² + ζ₂²), i.e. equal *translational* mean vis viva per molecule, from which "equal volumes of different gases at equal pressures and temperatures contain equal numbers of molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The internal share is carried along by each species at its own β, and "the value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them" — formalized later as β = (2/3)·1/(γ − 1) [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

So the theory keeps the two vis vivas separate in bookkeeping (via β), treats only the translational part as what equalizes across species at thermal equilibrium, and leaves the internal part fixed by a molecule-specific ratio inferred from specific heats rather than computed from the dynamics — the corpus explicitly notes "we have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Question 7 [theory]

> What does the theory commit to about the persistence and conservation of total vis viva across encounters between molecules constituting a body?

### Answer (theory)

The corpus commits to conservation of total translational *vis viva* across encounters, with qualifications that depend on how molecules are constituted.

**For pure centres of force, conservation is strict.** The centre of gravity's motion is invariant — "The motion of the centre of gravity will not be altered by the mutual action of the molecules, of whatever nature that action may be" — and each molecule's speed relative to that centre is preserved in magnitude: "each will be moving with the same velocity relative to the centre of gravity that it had before the mutual action, but the direction of this relative velocity will be turned through an angle 2θ in the plane of the orbit" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Together these make total translational *vis viva* conserved per encounter.

This commitment is made explicit in the final-distribution derivation, where the *only* constraint linking pre- and post-encounter speeds is

> "$M_1 a^2 + M_2 b^2 = M_1 a'^2 + M_2 b'^2$" (eq. 23).

**For molecules with rotation or internal vibration, strict per-encounter conservation of translational *vis viva* is not assumed.** Maxwell explicitly weakens it to an average commitment:

> "we must content ourselves with the assumption that the value of θ is, on an average, the same as for pure centres of force, and that the final velocities differ from the initial velocities only by quantities which may in each collision be neglected, although in a great many encounters the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

So translational *vis viva* trades with internal energy; per-encounter changes are treated as negligible; the long-run ratio (following Clausius) is β − 1.

**Between encounters**, *vis viva* persists unchanged: paths are "sensibly rectilinear and beyond the sphere of sensible action of the neighbouring molecules," and the free time is taken "so great compared with the time during which it is deflected … that we may neglect both the time and the distance described by the molecules during the encounter."

What the corpus does *not* commit to: exact per-encounter energy conservation in the presence of internal degrees of freedom, or any detailed mechanism for the internal/translational exchange — "We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules."

## Question 8 [theory]

> How does the theory treat the equilibrium condition between the translational and internal vis viva of a single species of molecule?

### Answer (theory)

# Equilibrium between translational and internal vis viva in a single species

The corpus does not *derive* this equilibrium; it posits it, following Clausius, and attaches an empirical handle to it through the specific heats.

## The posited ratio

Maxwell introduces β as the ratio of total molecular energy to energy of translation:

> "in all other cases the whole energy of the molecule may be represented by ½Mv²β, where β is the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation. The ratio β will be different for every molecule, and will be different for the same molecule after every encounter with another molecule, but it will have an average value depending on the nature of the molecules, as has been shown by Clausius. The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

So the equilibrium condition is that the *mean* ratio β settles to a fixed value characteristic of the molecular species, even though the ratio fluctuates molecule-by-molecule and encounter-by-encounter.

## The mechanism: repeated small exchanges

The corpus is explicit that the exact internal dynamics of an encounter cannot be solved, so the approach to equilibrium is assumed, not computed:

> "We have no means of determining such intricate actions in the present state of our knowledge of molecules, so that we must content ourselves with the assumption that the value of θ is, on an average, the same as for pure centres of force, and that the final velocities differ from the initial velocities only by quantities which may in each collision be neglected, although in a great many encounters the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

A single collision is treated as elastic for the purposes of the deflection integral; the translational–internal equilibration is treated as a slow drift accomplished by many such collisions.

## The equilibrium relation used downstream

Once equilibrium is invoked, the mean internal energy per molecule is tied rigidly to the translational agitation:

> "If we assume with Clausius that the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value (β − 1), we may conclude that, except in very violent disturbances, this ratio is always preserved, so that
> Ē = (β − 1)(ξ̄² + η̄² + ζ̄²)." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

This lets the total heat per unit volume be written

> "½β(ξ̄² + η̄² + ζ̄²)ρ, or ³⁄₂ βp" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

and β is then fixed from experiment via

> "γ = (2 + 3β)/(3β), whence β = ⅔ · 1/(γ−1)." [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

## What the corpus does *not* do

- It does not derive β from a model of internal molecular structure; pure centres of force are noted as a limiting case with no rotational energy at all.
- It does not give a relaxation time for the translational–internal equilibration analogous to the relaxation time T for shear stress; the exchange is simply assumed to be rapid compared with "violent disturbances."
- For a pure centre of force, the treatment collapses: "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The equilibrium question only arises for molecules with internal degrees of freedom.

In short: the corpus treats the translational/internal equilibrium as a Clausius-style postulate — a stable mean ratio 1 : (β − 1) reached by many small collisional exchanges, with β taken from measured specific-heat ratios rather than computed from first principles.

## Question 9 [theory]

> What does the theory predict about the sharing of vis viva between two bodies of different constitution brought into thermal contact?

### Answer (theory)

# What the theory predicts about sharing of *vis viva* between bodies of different constitution in thermal contact

## The central result: equal mean *vis viva* per molecule

The corpus states that when two systems of molecules interact freely, the mean translational *vis viva* per molecule becomes equal across the two systems. Maxwell derives this twice — once by a direct argument about pressure and temperature, and once from the final distribution of velocities.

From the direct argument: "the final result in the case of hard elastic bodies is to cause the average *vis viva* of a molecule to be the same for all the different kinds of molecules... When the flow of *vis viva* from the one kind of molecules to the other is zero, the temperature is said to be the same" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

From the final distribution (equations 33–34): "When there are two systems of molecules $M_1\alpha^2 = M_2\beta^2$, whence $M_1\overline{v_1^2} = M_2\overline{v_2^2}$, or the mean *vis viva* of a molecule will be the same in each system" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Independence from the law of force

Maxwell emphasizes this is a constitution-independent result: "This is a very important result in the theory of gases, and it is independent of the nature of the action between the molecules, as are all the other results relating to the final distribution of velocities" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. It holds "whatever theory we adopt as to the nature of the action between the individual molecules."

## Approach to equilibrium

For two gases in contact, the difference in mean *vis viva* per molecule decays exponentially. With $Q_1 = \frac{M_1}{M_1+M_2}(\overline{\xi_1^2}+\overline{\eta_1^2}+\overline{\zeta_1^2})$ and analogously $Q_2$, Maxwell derives $Q_2 - Q_1 = Ce^{-nt}$, so "if the gases are in contact and undisturbed, $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ will rapidly become equal. Now the state into which two bodies come by exchange of invisible agitation is called equilibrium of heat or equality of temperature" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Consequence: Law of Equivalent Volumes

Equality of mean molecular *vis viva*, combined with the kinetic expression for pressure, yields Gay-Lussac's law: "if the pressures as well as the temperatures be the same in two gases, $\frac{M_1}{\rho_1} = \frac{M_2}{\rho_2}$, or the masses of the individual molecules are proportional to the density of the gas" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Role of internal degrees of freedom

The *vis viva* that equalizes is the translational part (motion of the centre of gravity). Internal motion (rotation, vibration) is treated via Clausius's assumption that "the ratio of the mean energy of internal motion to that of agitation tends continually towards a definite value $(\beta - 1)$" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], so total molecular energy is $\frac{1}{2}\beta M v^2$ with $\beta$ depending on the molecule's constitution. Different constitutions can therefore carry different *total* energies per molecule at the same temperature, even though translational *vis viva* per molecule is equal.

## Extension beyond gases — explicitly tentative

Maxwell extends the principle cautiously: "A law of the same general character is probably to be found connecting the temperatures of liquid and solid bodies with the energy possessed by their molecules, although our ignorance of the nature of the connexions between the molecules renders it difficult to enunciate the precise form of the law" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. So for bodies that are not both gases, the corpus asserts only that *some* similar energy-sharing law likely governs equilibrium; the precise form is not given.

## Summary

For two gases of different constitution brought into thermal contact, the theory predicts that exchange of motion through molecular encounters drives the mean translational *vis viva* per molecule to a common value — this equality *defines* equality of temperature, is independent of the intermolecular force law, and entails the Law of Equivalent Volumes. For liquids or solids, the corpus conjectures an analogous law but does not specify it.

## Question 10 [theory]

> What must hold about β for a system of perfectly smooth elastic spheres, and what does this reveal about the constitution such a model assumes?

### Answer (theory)

**β for perfectly smooth elastic spheres**

Maxwell defines β as "the ratio of the total energy to the energy of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The corpus does not name "perfectly smooth elastic spheres" as a distinct case, but the relevant reasoning is supplied for an adjacent one:

> "If the molecules are pure centres of force, there can be no energy of rotation, and the whole energy is reduced to that of translation" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

For smooth spheres the same conclusion follows by inference: collisions transmit force only along the line of centres, so no torque acts, and rotational energy cannot be communicated through encounters. Whatever rotation a sphere happens to carry is decoupled from the translational thermal equilibration described by equations (4)–(7) and (36)–(43). Under Maxwell's thermalized ratio, β = 1.

**Consequence via specific heats**

From the corpus,

> "γ = (2+3β)/(3β), whence β = (2/3)(1/(γ−1))" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]

β = 1 forces γ = 5/3.

**What this reveals about the assumed constitution**

The smooth-elastic-sphere model tacitly assumes a molecule with no internal degree of freedom that can thermalize with translation — mechanically indistinguishable from a point, or from a body whose internal motions are inaccessible to collision. Maxwell is explicit that real molecules may instead be "systems of several such centres of force, bound together by their mutual actions" or "small solid bodies of a determinate form" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], in which case "the energy of translation and the internal energy of the molecules arrive, by repeated small exchanges, at a final ratio, which we shall suppose to be that of 1 to β − 1" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases], so β > 1.

He notes that β is fixed empirically: "The value of β can be determined if we know either of the specific heats of the gas, or the ratio between them" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. His datum "For air we have γ = 1.409" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases] yields β ≈ 1.63, and γ = 1.27 for carbonic acid yields β ≈ 2.47 — both incompatible with β = 1. The smooth-sphere constitution is therefore a limiting fiction that real gases violate: they carry invisible agitation beyond translation, which a smooth sphere has no mechanism to receive or surrender.

## What Distinguishes Simple from Compound Bodies

The authors draw the line operationally, not philosophically. For simple bodies, atomic weights can be derived from measured combination ratios: "the relative weights of their atoms are deduced from the ratios observed between the weights of the elementary substances that unite together." There remains "something arbitrary in the choice of the specific weight of the elementary molecules," but only within "two or three numbers which have the most simple relation to each other" — a bounded ambiguity they resolve by appeal to chemical analogy.

For compound bodies, the situation is worse: "no precise method exists of discovering the real number of atoms of each kind which enter into a combination," making compound atomic weights "purely arbitrary." Because the product (specific heat × atomic weight) is the key quantity in the law, an undetermined atomic weight makes the product uninterpretable. The authors therefore set compounds aside explicitly: "we choose rather to wait till the new order of considerations which we have just established can be applied to a sufficiently great number of bodies... that the opinion adopted may be founded on decisive conclusions."

Compound bodies mentioned but not tabulated include water, sulphuret of lead, oxides of copper/lead/silver, carburet of sulphur, nitric acid, and nitre. All appear only in qualitative discussion of heat released during combination, not in the specific-heat table [1819-dulong-petit].

## Question 15 [evidence]

> Under what measurement conditions (temperature interval above ambient, pressure of residual air, mass of sample, duration of cooling) were the cooling-rate observations performed, and how uniform were these conditions across substances?

### Answer (evidence)

All four values appear explicitly in `[1819-dulong-petit]`:

**Temperature interval above ambient:** 10° to 5° centigrade above the ambient medium. This window was held constant across all substances — operating at the same temperature eliminated errors from thermometer graduation irregularities.

**Pressure of residual air:** The air in the enclosing vessel was evacuated until its tension "did not exceed two millimetres" (millimetres of mercury). This was reproduced identically for each experiment.

**Mass of sample:** For the densest substances (gold, platinum), masses "did not exceed the weight of 30 grammes." No lower bound or per-substance mass table is given; 30 g is stated as an upper limit for the most challenging cases.

**Duration of cooling:** In the fastest-cooling cases, duration "was not less than 15 minutes." Again, this is a lower bound on the worst case, not a per-substance table.

**Uniformity across substances:** Conditions were designed to be structurally identical for every run: same temperature interval, same vacuum level, same blackened ice-walled surrounding vessel, same silver cylindrical sample container with the thermometer reservoir along its axis. The authors state that agreement between their cooling-rate results and independent mixture/calorimeter measurements for the poorest conductors confirmed the method's consistency. No substance-by-substance variation in conditions is reported — the paper presents only summary bounds, not per-run logs.

## Question 17 [evidence]

> What empirical evidence does the corpus cite for the claim that compound bodies show "a very simple ratio" between their capacity and that of their elementary constituents?

### Answer (evidence)

The corpus provides **no numerical evidence** for this claim.

Dulong and Petit assert the simple-ratio law for compounds, but explicitly defer the supporting data:

> "We shall satisfy ourselves with saying, that in abstracting every particular supposition, the **observations which we have hitherto made tend to establish this remarkable law**; viz. that there always exists a very simple ratio between the capacity of the compound atoms and that of the elementary atoms."

They explain why no table appears: the atomic weight problem for compounds is unsolved. For elementary bodies they can divide specific heat by atomic weight to get per-atom capacity (their 13-element table with products clustering near 0.374). For compounds they cannot do the analogous calculation without knowing how many atoms of each kind enter the combination — which they call "purely arbitrary" — so they withhold results pending more decisive experiments.

The only named compound measurements in the paper are qualitative comparisons about **heat of combination**, not specific-heat ratios: water (oxygen + hydrogen) and sulphuret of lead (sulphur + lead) are cited to argue that heat evolved during combination is unrelated to capacity change — a different point entirely.

The gases oxygen, azote, and hydrogen are mentioned via Laroche and Berard's measurements, but those are elementary substances, not compounds.

**Bottom line**: `[1819-dulong-petit]` contains no numerical table, no measured values, and no quantified ratios for compound bodies. The simple-ratio claim for compounds is stated as a conjecture supported by unspecified private observations, explicitly flagged as preliminary and awaiting publication.
