## Question 18 [evidence]

> What does the corpus report about whether specific heat depends on the physical form or state of aggregation of a body, and what measurements support or qualify this?

The corpus makes one direct claim on this question and supports it with limited numerical evidence.

**The claim:** "The law, which we have announced, appears to be independent of the form which the bodies affect, provided always that we consider them in the same circumstances." [1819-dulong-petit]

**Supporting evidence — gases:**
Dulong and Petit cite the gas specific heat measurements of Laroche and Berard. For oxygen and azotic (nitrogen) gas, the reported values "do not differ from what they ought to be to agree accurately with our law, except by a quantity less than the probable errors of such experiments." No exact numerical values for these gases are quoted in the corpus; the agreement is characterized qualitatively as within experimental error. [1819-dulong-petit]

**Qualification — hydrogen:**
Hydrogen's value is described as "rather too small." The authors attribute this not to a genuine form-dependence but to an experimental artifact: hydrogen cools so rapidly relative to other elastic fluids that the measurement method introduced an uncorrected inaccuracy. They state the discrepancy can be explained without "any forced supposition." No corrected numerical value is provided. [1819-dulong-petit]

**Methodological note:**
The solid-body measurements in the table were all conducted on substances in "a state of very fine powder" under low pressure (tension not exceeding two millimetres). The corpus gives no measurements on the same substance in multiple physical states; the form-independence claim rests entirely on cross-substance comparison between the solids table and Laroche and Berard's gas data.

The corpus does not provide measurements that directly compare a single element across solid, liquid, and gas phases.
