## Question 20 [evidence]

> What does the corpus record about the relationship between interatomic distances, density, and dilatability — specifically the Gay-Lussac observation on carburet of sulphur and alcohol at their boiling points?

The corpus addresses this in the closing paragraph of [1819-dulong-petit], but provides no numerical values for any of the quantities involved.

The recorded claims are:

1. **Proposed relation**: Dulong and Petit assert it is "very probable that there exists a simple relation between the dilatability of liquids and the distances of their particles." No formula or coefficients are given.

2. **Gay-Lussac observation**: Gay-Lussac observed "the identity of the contractions of the carburet of sulphur and alcohol, setting out from their respective boiling points." The corpus records this as a qualitative finding — identical contraction behavior — not a quantitative one. No contraction values, temperatures, or units are cited.

3. **Interparticle distance claim**: Dulong and Petit interpret Gay-Lussac's result as evidence that "at the temperatures in which they were compared, the distances between their particles are almost exactly the same." Again, no numerical values or units are given.

The corpus supplies no boiling points, no contraction magnitudes, no distance estimates, and no density figures for either substance. The Gay-Lussac observation is cited as corroborating support for the dilatability-distance conjecture, but all statements remain qualitative.
