## Question 5 [theory]

> What does elastic collision require of the constituent bodies, and what does this commitment say about their fundamental nature?

# What elastic collision requires, and what that commitment says about the molecule

## The commitment as Herapath made it

Treating collision as the rebound of "perfectly hard bodies" — what Maxwell calls the hypothesis of "elastic spheres of definite radius" — requires the constituents to have definite extension, rigidity, and impenetrability. Maxwell identifies this commitment as decisively broken on two counts.

First, it violates the relativity of motion. Herapath's "theory of the collisions of perfectly hard bodies, such as he supposes the molecules to be, is faulty, inasmuch as it makes the result of impact depend on the absolute motion of the bodies, so that by experiments on such hard bodies (if we could get them) we might determine the absolute direction and velocity of the motion of the earth" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. A collision law that lets absolute motion leak out through experiment cannot be correct.

Second, it gives the wrong temperature dependence for viscosity. Hard elastic molecules "would make it vary as the square root of the absolute temperature"; experiment shows viscosity proportional to the absolute temperature, which "requires us to abandon this hypothesis ... and to adopt the hypothesis of a repulsive force inversely as the fifth power of the distance between the molecules, which is the only law of force which gives the observed result" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## What the hard-sphere commitment implicitly demands

If one insists on extended solid bodies as molecules, Maxwell warns of a hidden cost: "if necessary, we may suppose them to be small solid bodies of a determinate form; but in this case we must assume a new set of forces binding the parts of these small bodies together, and so introduce a molecular theory of the second order" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. Rigidity is not free — it presupposes an inner binding law, pushing the problem down a level.

## What this says about the fundamental nature of the molecule

Maxwell draws the conclusion explicitly: the macroscopic attributes we associate with "bodies" need not attach to molecules at all. "The doctrines that all matter is extended, and that no two portions of matter can coincide in the same place, being deductions from our experiments with bodies sensible to us, have no application to the theory of molecules" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

Accordingly, the molecule is redescribed as whatever "moves about as a single body": "These molecules may be mere points, or pure centres of force endowed with inertia, or the capacity of performing work while losing velocity. They may be systems of several such centres of force, bound together by their mutual actions, and in this case the different centres may either be separated, so as to form a group of points, or they may be actually coincident, so as to form one point" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

The "collision," on this reading, is not an impact of surfaces but a deflection in a central orbit: "If we regard the molecules as simple centres of force, then each molecule will describe a plane curve about this centre of gravity, and the two curves will be similar to each other and symmetrical with respect to the line of apses" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases]. The encounter is specified by the relative velocity $V$, the offset $b$, and the plane angle $\varphi$; the outcome is the deflection $2\theta$, determined once the law of force is given.

Maxwell underscores how little the observable consequences constrain the underlying picture: "The same result and the same ratio of the elasticities would be obtained if we supposed the molecules to be at rest, and to act on one another with forces depending on the distance, as in the statical molecular theory of elasticity. The coincidence of the properties of a medium in which the molecules are held in equilibrium by attractions and repulsions, and those of a medium in which the molecules move in straight lines without acting on each other at all, deserves notice from those who speculate on theories of physics" [1867-maxwell-dynamical-theory-of-gases].

## Summary of the commitment

In the corpus, the demand for literal elastic collision commits one to extended, impenetrable, rigid bodies — and this commitment (i) contradicts the relativity of motion, (ii) contradicts the measured temperature law of viscosity, and (iii) requires an unexplained inner binding theory. Maxwell's response is to drop the commitment: the molecule's fundamental nature is that of a centre (or system of centres) of force endowed with inertia, and "collision" is a deflection governed by a continuous law of force — for the gases treated here, a repulsion inversely as the fifth power of distance.
