### TA5-SIG: sig(t) is not shown to be a well-defined function — uniqueness of max(S) is not established
**Class**: REVISE
**Foundation**: NAT-order (NatStrictTotalOrder) — exactly-one trichotomy Consequence, antisymmetry of ≤
**ASN**: TA5-SIG (LastSignificantPosition), nonzero case — "NAT-wellorder applied to U delivers a least upper bound m ... we set sig(t) = m = max(S)"
**Issue**: NAT-wellorder's least-element principle delivers existence: `(E m ∈ U :: (A n ∈ U :: m ≤ n))`. It does not assert uniqueness. For sig to be a well-defined operator T → ℕ, max(S) must be uniquely determined — otherwise "we set sig(t) = m" picks among possibly many candidates and the definition is not a function. Uniqueness of min(U) is derivable: if m and m' are both minimal in U, then m ≤ m' (m is a lower bound, m' ∈ U) and m' ≤ m (symmetrically), so m = m' by antisymmetry of ≤. Antisymmetry follows directly from NAT-order's exactly-one trichotomy — the same resource already cited by TA5-SIG. But the argument is absent. By contrast, T0's comprehension axiom explicitly marks uniqueness via "extensionality makes this t unique," and NAT-card's axiom reads "the unique k ∈ ℕ." TA5-SIG provides neither the explicit marker nor the uniqueness derivation, leaving sig's function-hood unestablished.
**What needs resolving**: The proof must establish that min(U) is unique — either by invoking antisymmetry of ≤ (derivable from the already-cited NAT-order's exactly-one trichotomy) directly, or by asserting uniqueness in the definition slot analogously to NAT-card's "the unique k."
