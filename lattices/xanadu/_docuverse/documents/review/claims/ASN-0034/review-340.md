### TumblerAdd's NAT-cancel Depends does not specify which summand-absorption form is used
**Foundation**: NAT-cancel (NatAdditionCancellation) — states four independent clauses: left cancellation, right cancellation, summand absorption standard form `m + n = m ⟹ n = 0`, and summand absorption symmetric form `n + m = m ⟹ n = 0`. The claim body explicitly argues: "Both summand-absorption forms are stated independently because T0's NAT-* enumeration does not include commutativity of addition on ℕ, so neither form is derivable from the other."
**ASN**: TumblerAdd's Depends entry: "NAT-cancel (NatAdditionCancellation) — summand absorption rules out `aₖ + wₖ = wₖ` in the dominance sub-case `aₖ > 0`." The proof text at this site: "NAT-cancel's summand absorption rules out equality (which would force `aₖ = 0`)". The equality being ruled out is `aₖ + wₖ = wₖ`, which matches the *symmetric* form `n + m = m ⟹ n = 0` at `n = aₖ, m = wₖ` (yielding `aₖ = 0`); the standard form `m + n = m ⟹ n = 0` does not fit this shape without commutativity, which NAT-cancel has explicitly declined to admit.
**Issue**: T1's NAT-cancel Depends entry names its clause precisely ("right cancellation at `1`, used in sub-case (ii, ii) to pass from `m + 1 = n + 1` to `m = n`"). TumblerAdd, at a site where the symmetric/standard distinction is exactly the distinction NAT-cancel's body takes care to make un-bridgeable without commutativity, cites only "summand absorption." A reader following TumblerAdd's Depends into NAT-cancel cannot tell which of the two independent absorption clauses is consumed, and NAT-cancel's own prose warns that picking the wrong one would require commutativity that the axiom bundle withholds. The precision gap lands precisely where NAT-cancel's own design argues it must not.
**What needs resolving**: Either refine TumblerAdd's NAT-cancel Depends entry to name the symmetric form `n + m = m ⟹ n = 0` (with the `n = aₖ, m = wₖ` instantiation), or rewrite the dominance sub-case so the equality `aₖ + wₖ = wₖ` is ruled out through content already named without ambiguity (e.g., by reducing it to a right-cancellation or standard-absorption step at the precise shape those clauses cover).
