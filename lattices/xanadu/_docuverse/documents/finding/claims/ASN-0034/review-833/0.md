### T10a.5 — lowest common ancestor existence asserted without grounding
**Class**: REVISE
**Foundation**: NAT-wellorder (NatWellOrdering) — least-element principle; the bounded-maximum construction in TA5-SIG (LastSignificantPosition) demonstrates the exact pattern required
**ASN**: T10a (AllocatorDiscipline), Consequence 5 proof, opening paragraph: "Let X and Y be allocators not in an ancestor-descendant relationship. They share a lowest common ancestor P; let Cₓ and Cᵧ be the children of P on the paths to X and Y. Cₓ ≠ Cᵧ by minimality of P."
**Issue**: "They share a lowest common ancestor P" is an existential claim about the deepest element in the intersection of the two ancestor chains. The allocator tree axiom establishes that each non-root node has a unique parent and that depth is a finite natural number per node, so the root is always a common ancestor. But identifying the *lowest* (deepest) such common ancestor requires finding the maximum of a bounded nonempty set of natural numbers — specifically the set of depths `{d : ∃P, P is a common ancestor of X and Y at depth d}`, which is nonempty (contains 0) and bounded above by min(depth(X), depth(Y)). Finding the maximum of a bounded subset of ℕ requires the upper-bound set construction and NAT-wellorder, exactly as TA5-SIG derives max(S) from NAT-wellorder applied to the set of upper bounds of S. That argument is not given here, and NAT-wellorder does not appear in T10a's Depends list. The tree structure alone — unique parent per non-root node, finite depth — does not supply the maximum-existence inference; it supplies only that the intersection is finite and nonempty.
**What needs resolving**: Either (a) supply the LCA existence derivation — form the set of depths at which X and Y share a common ancestor, apply the upper-bound-set construction from NAT-wellorder to obtain its maximum (mirroring TA5-SIG's pattern), and add NAT-wellorder to T10a's Depends; or (b) restructure the T10a.5 proof to avoid the LCA invocation, for example by strong induction on depth(X) + depth(Y), establishing the divergence-position argument without appealing to the ungrounded existence claim.

VERDICT: REVISE
