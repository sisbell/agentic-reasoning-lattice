### "Forces k to be the least element … supplied by NAT-wellorder" conflates two distinct claims
**Foundation**: NAT-wellorder (least-element principle), T3
**ASN**: Divergence, uniqueness paragraph: *"Case (i)'s value `k` is unique: `aₖ ≠ bₖ ∧ (A i : 1 ≤ i < k : aᵢ = bᵢ)` forces `k` to be the least element of `S := {i ∈ ℕ : 1 ≤ i ∧ i ≤ #a ∧ i ≤ #b ∧ aᵢ ≠ bᵢ}`, supplied by NAT-wellorder."*
**Issue**: NAT-wellorder supplies the *existence* of a least element of a nonempty subset of ℕ; it does not "force `k` to be the least element." The uniqueness of `k` inside case (i) is a direct consequence of the conjunction `aₖ ≠ bₖ ∧ (A i : 1 ≤ i < k : aᵢ = bᵢ)` — any two values satisfying this would each be forced to equal the other's strict lower bound, producing a contradiction without invoking NAT-wellorder. NAT-wellorder is needed separately to guarantee case (i) *has* a witness when its hypothesis "there exists `k` with a shared-position mismatch" is recast from "some shared-position mismatch exists" to "the least such index exists." Collapsing these two roles into one sentence hides which axiom does which job.
**What needs resolving**: Separate (a) the uniqueness argument (from the case (i) characterization, no axiom needed beyond equality reasoning) from (b) the appeal to NAT-wellorder (used to extract the least mismatch index from the nonempty set `S` when recasting the case (i) hypothesis as an existence statement about the least such `i`).
